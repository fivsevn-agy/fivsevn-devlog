Iran will no longer accept endless talks. It is creating deterrence on its own terms

Iran will no longer accept endless talks. It is creating deterrence on its own terms Submitted by Seyed Hossein Mousavian on Mon, 06/08/2026 - 16:38 Weekend strikes highlight Tehran's shifting strategic calculus - and a refusal to remain bound by one-sided agreements Iranian pro-government demonstrators wave the flags of Iran and Lebanon’s Hezbollah movement, in Tehran on 7 June 2026 (Atta Kenare/AFP) On As conflict flared up again this weekend between Israel and Iran , negotiations with the US have failed to produce any agreement. Neither side can afford another war, but the path towards a diplomatic settlement has been stymied by Washington’s demands for far-reaching concessions from Iran, without any offer of commensurate reciprocal measures, such as the release of even a portion of Iran’s frozen assets. After the US and Israeli military strikes on Iran in 2025 and again this year, western discussions largely focused on the physical damage inflicted on Iran’s military and nuclear infrastructure, its uranium enrichment capabilities, and its stockpile of highly enriched uranium.&nbsp; In Iran, however, the central question is different: did negotiations and nuclear restraint produce greater security, or did they ultimately create heightened vulnerability? The reality is that the US-Israeli wars on Iran inflicted significant damage on its nuclear and military facilities. But the ongoing conflict has also imposed enormous costs on the US - including more than $1 trillion in expenditures, significant damage to US military assets, global economic disruption, and substantial civilian and military casualties. At the same time, the US-Israeli campaign has achieved few of its stated objectives, namely to eliminate Iran’s missile capabilities, end its nuclear programme, and facilitate political change. What the wars did change, however, was Iran’s strategic calculus, producing four major shifts in this regard.&nbsp; Firstly, we have seen the collapse of a strategy built on restraint and engagement. Despite a broad consensus among observers that Iran had complied with the 2015 nuclear deal , accepting extensive restrictions and unprecedented inspections, the US unilaterally withdrew from the agreement just three years later, and the military attacks followed.&nbsp; As a result, confidence in the idea that diplomacy and nuclear restraint can guarantee security has been severely damaged. For many Iranians, restraint is now increasingly viewed not as protection, but as vulnerability. Central question Secondly, public trust in the US has collapsed. While Iran’s leadership has long been sceptical of Washington, public opinion was often different.&nbsp; After the signing of the nuclear agreement a decade ago, optimism in Iran was widespread, with a Gallup survey at the time finding that 68 percent of Iranians believed their leaders had negotiated a good deal, 66 percent expected economic improvement, and 51 percent anticipated better relations with the US. Today, the debate inside Iran is no longer primarily about centrifuges or enrichment levels. The central question has become: if Iran accepts new restrictions, what guarantees exist that a future US administration will not abandon the agreement, or that another military confrontation will not follow? For many Iranians, the current crisis is less a nuclear dispute than a crisis of trust. Even voices that previously advocated de-escalation now emphasise the need for credible deterrent capabilities Thirdly, we have seen a shift from ideology to nationalism. For decades, confrontation with the US and Israel was framed primarily in ideological terms. The recent conflict appears to have produced a different dynamic: while many Iranians remain opposed to war, sanctions and isolation, public sentiment has increasingly shifted towards a form of everyday nationalism . Rather than strengthening ideological narratives, external military pressure has reinforced broader feelings of national identity and collective solidarity. This trend may become one of the most enduring political consequences of the wars. Finally, perhaps the most important shift concerns deterrence . Even voices that previously advocated de-escalation now emphasise the need for credible deterrent capabilities. This does not necessarily imply support for nuclear weapons; rather, it reflects a growing belief that no political agreement can remain sustainable unless Iran possesses sufficient means to deter future attacks. This shift played out in real-time over the weekend, as Iran launched strikes against Israel over the latter’s continuing assault on Tehran’s ally Lebanon - marking the first time Iran has hit Israel not as retaliation for attacks on its own territory, but as a warning over continuing ceasefire violations in Lebanon. Strategic framework Following the Iran- Iraq War in the 1980s, Iran’s security doctrine rested on three pillars:&nbsp;strengthening indigenous military capabilities (which eventually produced the missile, drone and cyber capabilities displayed in the recent conflict), achieving self-sufficiency in nuclear technology and domestic fuel production, and extending deterrence beyond Iran’s borders through its regional “axis of resistance”. When it comes to shaping Iran’s future security doctrine, the strategic impact of the 2025 and 2026 wars may ultimately exceed even that of Saddam Hussein’s invasion, since the US-Israeli assaults were widely perceived inside Iran as direct threats to national survival and sovereignty.&nbsp; Consequently, a revised strategic framework appears to be emerging around four principles. The first can be summarised as “security for all or security for none”. After the 2025 US-Israeli attacks, Iran’s military response was largely confined to Israel and a single American base in the Gulf.&nbsp; During the 2026 conflict, however, Iran’s strategic calculations expanded to include US military installations across the Gulf, the Strait of Hormuz, and broader global economic interests. From Tehran’s perspective , the lesson is clear: security can no longer be treated as a unilateral privilege. Either there is security for all regional actors, or security for none. Secondly, we have seen the emergence of a new key factor: public opinion. Before the wars, Iran’s strategic debate largely revolved around two concepts: the “battlefield” and “diplomacy”. The recent wars added a third: the street .&nbsp; The wave of nationalism that emerged was reflected in large public gatherings across major cities. While supporting national defence, many participants also conveyed a message to policymakers that excessive trust in negotiations with Washington is no longer acceptable. As a result, Iranian diplomacy today increasingly operates under the influence of both military institutions and public opinion. A broad consensus is emerging inside Iran that security, trust, deterrence and diplomacy are inseparable Thirdly, the emerging consensus emphasises deterrence through a combination of instruments: stronger military capabilities, preservation of nuclear expertise, continued regional partnerships, and incorporation of the Strait of Hormuz into broader security calculations. The result is a more comprehensive understanding of deterrence than the one that existed before the wars. Finally, the loss of Iran’s supreme leader, military commanders, regional partners and hundreds of civilians has created a powerful collective memory that will shape Iranian perceptions for years to come. Ayatollah Ali Khamenei was not only the leader of a state; he was also one of the most prominent Shia religious authorities, with millions of followers worldwide.&nbsp; For the first time in modern history, a leading Shia religious authority was killed in military action by foreign states. This is not an event that many Iranians or Shia communities around the world are likely to forget. After the killing of General Qassem Soleimani in 2020, US officials openly expressed concerns over possible Iranian retaliation. Recent conflicts have led not only to the killings of numerous senior Iranian military leaders, but also to the assassinations of key figures within the broader “axis of resistance”, including Hezbollah chief Hassan Nasrallah and Hamas negotiator Ismail Haniyeh .&nbsp; It would be unwise for policymakers and security institutions to dismiss the potential long-term security consequences of these events. Window for diplomacy Meanwhile, despite widespread mistrust, diplomacy remains far from dead. Although some Iranian political figures fear that another military confrontation may be on the horizon, significant support for diplomacy still exists within Iran.&nbsp; What has changed is not the desire for negotiations, but the expectations surrounding any future agreement. If the nuclear deal focused primarily on nuclear restrictions, many in Tehran now argue that any future agreement must contain three key elements. Firstly, nonproliferation within the framework of the nuclear Non-Proliferation Treaty must protect Iran’s right to enrich uranium, while building confidence that Tehran will not pursue nuclear weapons. And thirdly, any deal needs to include credible assurances that this military conflict will not be repeated. Despite the deep mistrust generated by recent wars, diplomacy remains the only viable path forward - because neither Iran, nor the US, nor indeed the region as a whole can escape the realities of coexistence. The central challenge is in breaking a decades-long cycle of crisis, sanctions, negotiations, agreements, collapse and renewed conflict, which neither military force nor economic pressure has resolved.&nbsp; The most important consequence of the recent wars is not a shift in Iran’s relative strength, but a transformation in its understanding of security.&nbsp; A broad consensus is emerging inside Iran that security, trust, deterrence and diplomacy are inseparable. Unless Washington and its regional allies recognise this shift, future agreements are likely to remain temporary, while the cycle of confrontation continues. Leigh’s film, among the most commercially and critically successful of his career,is driven by the dogged efforts of Hortense Cumberbatch (Marianne Jean-Baptiste), a young, Black, introverted optometrist, to uncover and make contact with her birth mother, Cynthia (Brenda Blethyn), a blowzy, woebegone factory worker who is also, much to Hortense’s amazement, white. In the wake of her adoptive mother’s off-screen death, Hortense finds herself increasingly and willingly pulled into Cynthia’s lonely life and chaotic family, which includes another illegitimate adult daughter (Claire Rushbrook), whom Cynthia has raised and alienated, and an unfailingly altruistic brother, Maurice (Timothy Spall), whose wife (Phyllis Logan) is infertile and resentful of her sister-in-law. Other revelations are dragged into the light over the course of the film but it is the unknown existence and racial identity of Hortense that most pivotally determine the narrative of Secrets &amp; Lies , providing the film with its reason for being. In Leigh’s cinema, characters belong as much to the actors playing them as to the director himself. As the filmmaker himself has made clear , his rigorous, much-mythologized method famously enlists “the actor as a contributing artist, not merely an interpreter.” His films, like those of his closest forebear John Cassavetes, have frequently been misconstrued as taped improvisations, warped by the whims of its performers. It is true that, for most of Leigh’s projects, actors conceive their characters from scratch, sometimes drawing from a list of people that they have known or encountered in their own lives. But the actors then work separately and with Leigh for months to refine personalities, professions, habits, and pasts down to the most humdrum detail, until they can confidently claim to know this fictive person from the inside out. Once this incubation period is over, the actor begins collaborating with other actors enmeshed in the same process to create relationships, deepen dynamics, and braid together their histories. Through extended and unrecorded improvisations, on-site rehearsals, and Leigh’s own external plotting, a story gradually crystallizes, though a proper screenplay tends to be put on paper only after the film is completed. Leigh’s actors shape, influence, and—in the truest, most organic sense—co-author their characters but, once on set, the actions these characters take and the choices they make are negotiated between the performers and Leigh, whose “decision-making and interventions inform every film, right across the board.” 1 The result is a work molded by many minds, a film equally scavenged and structured, its freewheeling characters held within narrative and relational parameters imposed by Leigh. After studying acting at the Royal Academy of Dramatic Art, Leigh decisively turned to directing and derived his filmic practice from his experiences helming productions on the London stage, a foundation felt in the vivid, often volatile intensity of his actors’ efforts. As Leigh puts it, “To get everything right for that one moment on film, that’s what interests me. You want the spontaneity of the theater to happen at that white-hot moment when the camera is rolling.” 2 Although Leigh’s films have one foot planted in the quotidian realm of kitchen-sink drama, the majority of his actors have retained a relatively heightened manner of performance, an anti-naturalism, that tends to exceed our slippery frame of cinematic realism, awing some critics—Ian Buruma aptly described Leigh’s climaxes as “theater, but with the dangerous edge of reality”—and repelling others, who have accused Leigh of condescending to his lower-class characters, vis-à-vis his actors and their warts-and-all portrayals. Called on repeatedly to defend his approach, Leigh can occasionally undersell the tension he generates by unleashing his restive, larger-than-life players into existing places and letting their bursts of behavior transform the physical world into a proscenium. His characters are “real people, like you and me,” he has claimed, but this doesn’t remotely get at the ferment and volubility of his actors and all that is delightfully, maddeningly, and diversely too much about the people who tend to populate his ever-expanding Britannia. How, then, does the presence of a Black protagonist complicate the notion of a typical Mike Leigh character, as symbolized in the slovenly, spluttering form of David Thewlis’s Johnny, the irate white man stripped of charm and armed with a serrated, mile-a-minute wit in Naked , the 1993 film released directly before Secrets &amp; Lies ? And if a Mike Leigh performance is synonymous with vigorous, uninhibited expression, what might the reserve of a performer in a Mike Leigh film consequently signify about her character’s subjectivity? Given everything known about Leigh’s working methods, Hortense must be regarded as the creation of both the director and Marianne Jean-Baptiste, the RADA-trained actress who first imagined and invented her and whose muted yet emotionally variegated performance does not function as nor conform to our expectations of a typical Mike Leigh performance. Hortense’s blackness discernibly sets her apart from the film’s other main characters; as Jean-Baptiste notes , “She’s the subject [of the film] but is in the subtext” of all that is conveyed and concealed among its other denizens, her birth family. Yet Jean-Baptiste personifies and expands on the character’s difference in more artful ways, refusing to exceptionalize or oversimplify her. The performance instead hinges on modes of quiet and opacity that distinguish Jean-Baptiste from her fellow actors in the film and those of Leigh’s filmography. “The theory of difference is invaluable,” wrote Édouard Glissant in his appeal for the right to opacity, which doubles as an assertion of autonomy, on behalf of minorities. “But difference can still contrive to reduce things to the Transparent.” In Secrets &amp; Lies , Hortense fosters an unmistakable connection to the viewer yet eludes psychological transparency and refrains from complete knowability. It is Jean-Baptiste who, through subtle performance style and primary authorship of the character, carefully sets the terms by which Hortense will and will not be legible to those who surround her and those in the audience who are nonetheless magnetized to the actress’ every delicate move. *** Secrets &amp; Lies (Mike Leigh, 1996). The casting of the dark-skinned Marianne Jean-Baptiste as the mixed-race daughter of a white woman drew vocal dissension upon the release of Secrets &amp; Lies . “She didn’t look right,” wrote Karen Alexander in a decade-capping overview of Black British cinema in the 1990s, recognizing Jean-Baptiste’s significant talents but deeming her “phenotypically unconvincing” in the part. The performance anticipates this critique early on: when a too-kindly social worker (Lesley Manville) tells Hortense that it is perfectly feasible for her birth mother to be white, her reaction is halfway between a baffled scowl and a soundless scoff. Believing Jean-Baptiste as the daughter of Brenda Blethyn may require a suspension of disbelief for audiences seeking frictionless fidelity from Leigh’s film—not an altogether unreasonable expectation for a filmmaker who has resolutely stressed the verisimilitude of the social worlds he has chronicled. And yet this fidelity claim might be easier to overlook for those who are immediately in thrall to Jean-Baptiste’s mellow certitude in the role and her palpable, lived-in devotion to the character; the actress is not merely an inhabitant of Hortense’s reality but one of its chief architects. “People keep talking about the script, asking what attracted me to the role,” Jean-Baptiste said in 1996. “There was no role—what you’re attracted to is the process.” Jean-Baptiste’s preparation for Secrets &amp; Lies involved attending university several times a week for three months in order to fully understand Hortense’s optometry practice. The actress gravitated to the hidden minutiae of Leigh’s method, not just selecting the character’s professional, monochromatic wardrobe but also storing those clothes and intermittently residing in the apartment that would double as Hortense’s, finding herself able to account for all of the items in her trash. Through improvisations, she fleshed out conjoined backstories with fellow actors Michele Austin, who momentarily appears as Hortense’s friend, and Brian Bovell and Trevor Laird, whose roles as Hortense’s brothers were truncated after a subplot about the sale of their childhood home was excised from the final cut. Jean-Baptiste found possessing extensive knowledge about the character “liberating” in the sense that it “frees you up to just be” before the camera. This ease is discernible across Secrets &amp; Lies , where Jean-Baptiste is an unforced and self-effacing presence, her performance closer in spirit to the attentive if agonized reserve of Spall than the frenzied mannerisms of Blethyn, Rushbrook, and others. Then again, it is Spall’s Maurice whose torment is the focal point of the film’s purgative climax, in which he apoplectically brandishes the film’s title like a cudgel against his loved ones. Hortense is granted no such catharsis yet remains all the more fascinating for keeping her feelings and motivations so close to the vest. Jean-Baptiste reveals various shades of the character: her affability with her patients; her weary disinterest in her brothers’ briefly glimpsed squabbles; her rueful disappointment at mother’s sudden passing. But Hortense is mostly captured in contemplation, though the precise content of these musings is scarcely explicated. Quiet, something of a rare trait in Leigh’s films, is paramount to Jean-Baptiste’s power in the part. Hortense’s default manner evokes Kevin Quashie’s notion of quiet as an untapped form of expressivity within Black culture, one not synonymous with “silence or stillness,” but rather “a metaphor for the full range of one’s inner life—one’s desires, ambitions, hungers, vulnerabilities, fears.” Quashie continues: The idea of quiet, then, can shift attention to what is interior. This shift can feel like a kind of heresy if the interior is thought of as apolitical or inexpressive, which it is not: one’s inner life is raucous and full of expression, especially if we distinguish the term “expressive” from the notion of public. Indeed the interior could be understood as the source of human action—that anything we do is shaped by the range of desires and capacities of our inner life. Jean-Baptiste’s quiet facade embodies thought without clarifying its subject matter, suggesting the restless inquisitiveness that propels Hortense forward, in search of Cynthia. But what compels Hortense to continually entertain Cynthia’s company past the point of their excruciating introduction and to develop and nurture a bond with a woman who, despite their shared DNA, is Hortense’s polar opposite in innumerable ways? The loss of Hortense’s adoptive mother, with whom she attests to having a normal, loving relationship but whom she claims to never really have known, is too simplistic an explanation. Both Leigh and Jean-Baptiste avoid rationalizing Hortense along these lines; there are no expository monologues about the derangement of grief nor the loneliness of the adopted child. Ashley Clark argues that “the film doesn’t really explore how Hortense processes the monumental upending of her own identity, how she reacts to the newly complicated nature of her own blackness in relation to her existing family and community.” And yet this lack of exploration arguably befits a fundamentally ambivalent character. What informs Hortense’s desire to not just find but know her white mother is ungraspable and obscure, opaque in that it is a choice deprived of a single dramatically-expedient reason. An emblematic moment of the performance occurs in a car as Hortense drives Cynthia home following their first meeting. Hortense reveals to Cynthia that she has known about her adoption since childhood, when her late mother revealed it to her on a flight home from Barbados. “Were you upset?” Cynthia asks Hortense. The latter responds, in a tone at once evasive and matter-of-fact that she “just looked out at the clouds,” an elision that refuses to dictate the tenor of this pivotal moment in the character’s life and preserves the privacy of young Hortense’s response. Cynthia does not press the inquiry further and, even by the film’s end, we never totally grasp what being adopted might mean—or have once meant—to Hortense, how this knowledge (and the years spent holding this knowledge in the front or back of one’s mind) informs her search to locate Cynthia. This is neither the first or the last time she will elude apprehension. What matters to Hortense—and to Jean-Baptiste—is her quest to know, rather than be known. Per Glissant, “For the time being, perhaps, give up this old obsession with discovering what lies at the bottom of natures.” *** Secrets &amp; Lies (Mike Leigh, 1996). It was Jean-Baptiste’s decision to sit beside Blethyn during Hortense and Cynthia’s first meeting in an empty Central London tearoom, an awkward arrangement that gives Secrets &amp; Lies its centerpiece scene and one of its signature images, that of Black daughter and white mother elbow-to-elbow in a booth, faces forward, acutely alone in their shock and chagrin. Jean-Baptiste intuited that it would be easier for Hortense to show Cynthia the paperwork proving her parentage if the two were seated alongside each other. But the blocking would also spare Hortense the embarrassment of having to look at her disbelieving mother throughout the eight-minute conversation, shot in one unbroken take. A study in contrasts emerges during this extended two-shot, a divergence not solely of fictitious dispositions but of acting styles; it is the prototypical Mike Leigh performance sitting next to its inverse. Blethyn’s wide-eyed recollection of the repressed encounter that produced Hortense dominates the scene but the eye keeps shifting leftward to Jean-Baptiste and the nuanced portrait of bemused discomfort that she sketches here with her patient waiting, gentle prodding, and downward stares. The lachrymose fervor of Blethyn’s style is recognizably discordant with her costar’s equanimity and the stunning incompatibility of this pair does not augur a genuine connection between mother and daughter. Hortense’s solitude is keenest in the company of others. The clear exception is an outlying scene that is one of the film’s most beloved, a dialogue between Hortense and another Black Londoner, her longtime friend, Austin’s Dionne. Over dinner and wine, the duo discuss their sexual pursuits, their mothers, and the discontents of their upbringings, complete with Hortense’s comical impersonation of a Caribbean neighbor. Their banter is fast, free, and relaxed; at one point, the scrunching-up of lips is all they require to understand one another. This is a different Hortense—not necessarily a “realer” or more “authentic” one, but a looser and funnier, more unfiltered and meditative one. We will seldom see this side of Hortense again—except when she is with Cynthia. Once Hortense starts socializing with her mother, Jean-Baptiste complicates our assumptions about where—and with whom—the character can find comfort. Hortense’s characteristic reticence remains strikingly dissimilar from Cynthia’s too-muchness, but she is not only reticent. There is a girlish giddiness to their handful of outings, even a sense of delight that Jean-Baptiste conveys as she pulls faces, imitates Sylvester Stallone, and giggles at a hungry Cynthia’s malapropic declaration that “I’m ravishing!” What exactly is Hortense doing here? Feeding an outsider’s curiosity about this white woman and her working-class world? Enjoying the flattery that Cynthia doles out about Hortense’s beauty and professional success? Honoring a personal imperative to relate to the only one of her mothers still living? Leigh and Jean-Baptiste never resolve this query, which is why the growing geniality of the scenes between Hortense and Cynthia comes as such a surprise. And yet for all of their merriment, they are still women adjusting to implausible roles, and the self-conscious strain of their pauses say as much as their gasps of laughter. “Human behaviors are fractal in nature,” Glissant observed. Recall, too, Hortense’s complicated reaction when Cynthia finally identifies herself as her mother in that tearoom, the mixture of appreciation and unease that sifts through Jean-Baptiste’s face without settling into something straightforward. Her response is unfixed, as is her nature. *** The climax of Secrets &amp; Lies unfolds over the course of an explosive 21st birthday party for Cynthia’s daughter Roxanne, held at Maurice’s home. Hortense, pretending to be a workmate of Cynthia’s, attempts to use her politesse as a shield but only recedes further into herself, her discomfiture resembling a kind of nausea. (Notice that the gems hanging prominently from her ears are the same ones worn to her mother’s funeral.) When Cynthia, without warning, drops the bombshell about her newfound daughter, Hortense is forced to disclose her real identity. The character is not central to the recriminations and rapprochements that follow but becomes a silent, steely bystander to the threatened breakdown of her maternal family. Hortense noticeably chooses not to participate, an observer to the eruptions. At one point, she apathetically shrugs away Maurice’s concerned pats on the arm. It is only when Cynthia reveals to Roxane the long-withheld identity of her father, by her account “a nice man” studying abroad from America, that Hortense intervenes, coldly asking her mother, “Was my father a nice man?” The intentionally barbed question does its damage. But Hortense takes Cynthia in her arms, the embrace a mercy. She takes the path forward. Leigh’s film ends with Hortense welcomed into the family, testing the waters with Roxane in the backyard of the terraced house she shares with Cynthia. The pair admit to the weirdness of this arrangement but the mood between them remains light and airy as they opt to believe that something worthwhile and sustainable may be fostered between them. Cynthia’s reckless reveal and the pain subsequently restrained by Hortense are only minutes in the past. But Hortense, at least, is willing to see this experiment through. Hortense is not so much an enigma as an explorer, one whose motives are entirely her own, a seeker of connection who evades absolutes and declines to explain herself. Jean-Baptiste, never once raising her voice and hardly ever losing her composure, markedly expands our preconceptions about what a Mike Leigh performance might look and sound like. Other Leigh stalwarts (Ruth Sheen, Imelda Staunton) share Jean-Baptiste’s knack for unfurling a pause with currents of thought and feeling, but I can’t say that I wonder about their characters in the way I do Hortense. (I want to believe that she’d hit it off with Sally Hawkins’s Poppy, a zestier character but one who sends out similar rays of decency in 2008’s Happy-Go-Lucky .) Jean-Baptiste’s performance is a balancing act between actorly charisma and all too human ambiguity, between narratively-ordained blackness and a refusal to represent the Blackness of this particular British woman as innate, predetermined, monolithic. At all times, Jean-Baptiste protects and preserves the introversion, modesty, and quiet of the character she brought into being. In 1997, Jean-Baptiste became the first Black British actress to be nominated for an Academy Award for her performance in Secrets &amp; Lies . (Although Blethyn and Jean-Baptiste share virtually equal screen time, the latter was campaigned as a supporting actress. 3 ) That same year, the thirty-year-old actress was excluded from an all-white delegation of young actors invited by the organization British Screen to attend the Cannes Film Festival, where Leigh’s film had won the Palme d’Or the year prior. Jean-Baptiste did not let the slight go quietly, telling The Guardian , “It is a shame on Britain. I see myself as British and I want to be celebrated by Britain.” Despite the historic recognition, this slight and the subsequent paucity of substantial opportunities for Jean-Baptiste in the decades since her breakthrough are testaments to how habitually disregarded Black interiority remains onscreen. She has found heftier parts on the London stage, including a recent triumph in an Ivo Van Hove revival of Arthur Miller’s All My Sons . But the British film industry and Hollywood, which has kept the actress in workaday rotation since her move to the States in the 2000s, have failed to plumb her greater depths. The exception to this trajectory was facilitated through the director whom the actress has said she would pay to hire her. Almost 30 years after Secrets &amp; Lies , Jean-Baptiste reunited with Leigh for Hard Truths (2024), an unshakable showcase for the actress, whose middle-class housewife, the inaptly named Pansy, carries anger like a glove that has been superglued to the hand. Searching for a skirmish with strangers and loved ones alike, Pansy cannot help but pucker her lips in disgust at the faintest sign of contentment in a society that has, for reasons beyond her control, denied her all its comforts and joys. Jean-Baptiste badgers and bellows with a fury first hilarious then haunting, rattling the cage that a lifetime of dashed hopes has raised around Pansy. (She also rekindles and spikes the rapport first sparked with her Secrets &amp; Lies bestie Michele Austin, majestic as Pansy’s forbearing and magnanimous sister.) Operating light years away from the self-containment of her breakthrough role, Jean-Baptiste sets a new criterion in Leigh’s canon of modern misanthropes and delivers the sort of performance around which acting careers are immortalized. Still, it is Hortense that feels like the rarer feat, the figure easier to neglector underestimate. Jean-Baptiste looks largely unaffected by the templates of her costars and predecessors in Leigh’s ensembles. But she also seems impervious to the straitjacketing expectations of cinematic relatability that have narrowed our tolerance for those who are not instantly apprehensible, whose lives are not mirrors reflecting back our own existence with crystal clarity. The actress ensures that Hortense remains irreducible, a character who rivets less for what she reveals than for the promise that beneath her quiet reserve spins a world unto itself. 1. Times and Tides: Tsui Hark Discusses His Career

MUBI Notebook · 2026-06-04 08:00 UTC

Illustration by Alix Pentecost Farren. There are few blockbuster filmmakers who are as industrious as Tsui Hark. A leading figure of the Hong Kong New Wave who has scaled the highest peaks of his national cinema, this prodigious multi-hyphenate has amassed more than 200 credits as either director, producer, writer, occasional actor, or some combination of all four. Often described as China’s answer to Steven Spielberg, Tsui was responsible for bringing Hollywood-style special effects to Hong Kong—literally, in the case of Zu: Warriors from the Magic Mountain (1983), for which he flew in a number of VFX artists who had worked on Star Wars (1977) and Tron (1982). Tsui has always operated at the vanguard of cinematic technologies. To watch his post-millennial output is to be dazzled by the blockbuster possibilities of digital ones and zeroes. Like Spielberg, much of his work as director is confined to the past or the realms of fantasy, but unlike his American contemporary, whose work this century is suffused with political critique, Tsui’s most recent epics of historical ( The Battle at Lake Changjin , 2021) or literary ( Legends of the Condor Heroes: The Gallants , 2025) populism are pointedly careful not to upset the nationalist apple cart. (Ahead of our conversation, even my questions were rigorously vetted.) Back in the day, Tsui would throw such caution to the wind: picture the burning tank that features prominently in A Better Tomorrow III: Love and Death in Saigon (1989), released just four months after the Tiananmen Square Massacre; or the spiritual turmoil of China’s cultural icon Wong Fei-hung in Once Upon a Time in China (1991), the film that made Jet Li a superstar. Then there’s Dangerous Encounter – 1st Kind (1980), a startlingly nihilistic youth movie that was banned by the state Censorship Unit for its political violence. While Tsui has yet to make another film as confrontational as Dangerous Encounter , he has made up for it with the genre-hopping breadth of his output. As director, he leaps between comedy ( Aces Go Places 3 , 1984; The Chinese Feast , 1995) and time-bending romance ( Love in the Time of Twilight , 1995), between fantastical melodrama ( Green Snake , 1993), dizzying action ( The Blade , 1995; Time and Tide , 2000), and whatever Tri-Star (1996) is. As a producer, he quickly became notorious for his meddlesome oversight, while simultaneously scoring smash hit after smash hit. John Woo reaped the rewards of his stewardship with A Better Tomorrow (1986), its 1987 sequel, and The Killer (1989)—all produced by Tsui—before the pair fell out, while the legendary King Hu lasted barely a month on The Swordsman (1990) under Tsui’s watchful eye. Peking Opera Blues (Tsui Hark, 1986). Tsui is of an age at which his most notable films are receiving anniversary restorations, with longstanding gaps in his expansive filmography finally beginning to be filled on home video. His 1984 masterpiece Shanghai Blues screened in Cannes in 2024 for its 40th, while the uncut version—with missing elements sourced from Tsui’s personal VHS copy—of Dangerous Encounter – 1st Kind was recently released on Blu-ray in France. (A US disc that uses the film’s alternate title Don’t Play With Fire is imminent.) To great astonishment, last year also saw the news that the home video label Shout! Studios had somehow gained the keys to one of cinema’s holy grails: the Golden Princess library, whose knotty business arrangements had kept many Hong Kong classics out of distribution for decades. With the floodgates now open, a number of Tsui’s films as producer and director are finally beginning to see the light of day, including the gender-bending masterwork often cited as his greatest, Peking Opera Blues (1986). Add to that Criterion’s recent 4K release of The Blade , hot on the heels of their 2021 boxset of the Once Upon a Time in China films, and it’s steadily becoming a great time to be a fan of Tsui Hark’s endlessly creative cinematic visions. With my questions approved, I jumped on a video call with Tsui, whose energy belied his 75 years. Even with my allotted 60 minutes stretched to 75, an attempt at a career interview with so much ground to cover was probably a fool’s errand, and I ran out of time before getting to some personal favorites like his Jean-Claude Van Damme-starring, stylistic blitzkrieg Knock Off (1998), or his incandescent ghost story Missing (2008). Great company, Tsui spoke in English, and this interview has been edited for clarity. NOTEBOOK: Let’s start at the very beginning of your filmmaking career. I’m curious about your experience studying film in Texas. You were in the US for a number of years. How did that come about? TSUI HARK: Going to America was unusual. I didn’t have any money, so I was always in crisis. Every semester while I was in school there was a financial alarm. I would work for a summer and by the time I returned to school the money was already spent. I would always be looking for a part-time job, for somewhere to stay. It was a very unstable situation, and I was skeptical about whether I would be able to finish my studies. I was also concerned about how much I was learning. So in my senior year at university, one semester before graduation, I started working in New York City, at a place called New York Films. They were a documentary rental and production company that always seemed to be in financial difficulty. But I was happy to be earning a living working in film. One day I found out that I didn’t actually graduate from school; I didn’t get my degree. So I had to go back and finish my work. I thought a degree was very important, until later I found out that it didn’t matter at all. People look at your abilities rather than the record of your studies. A career in filmmaking means that you don’t have to worry about making a living, that you can spend your time dreaming about your own worlds. I went to the States because I wanted to get into the film industry. The best result would be that I could become a director. When I left school, I didn’t know whether I should return to New York, because I had a job there, or go back to Hong Kong. NOTEBOOK: Was the plan always to return to Hong Kong eventually to make films? TSUI: I went to America because it seemed like that’s where all the decisions were made, but I had to return to Hong Kong because that’s where all my ideas came from. Hong Kong was the source of my inspiration, it was in my blood. NOTEBOOK: You must have been watching a lot of American and world cinema as part of your studies. What were you especially fond of, and what effect do you think this exposure and training had on your sensibilities as a filmmaker? TSUI: You can’t exclude things and expect to become a unique and original entity. We are mutually influenced. Cinema began by someone experimenting with a series of projected images to create the illusion of reality. There’s so much history there that it’s impossible to feel that you are original, and the path you are on as a filmmaker is the sum total of all the emotions and experiences you’ve had as a viewer. Being yourself doesn’t mean that you have to exclude everybody else. You have all these wonderful directors that made movies that you really love. Are you influenced by them? Of course. But is your filmmaking really affected by them? I’m not sure. In my opinion, you can never do what they did. They did wonderful things that, even across your entire career, you can never hope to attain. You can spend your life loving their work, but sorry, it doesn’t mean that you can do the same things yourself. The films I loved at school weren’t the commercial, popular movies. I was much more influenced by underground, experimental cinema. This was the kind of work that had courage, and the boldness of spirit to fight against tradition. Then later you find out that this is just a process that you have to go through, and that you’ve learned from the process. But I do still love a lot of the films that I first encountered back then. I was very affected by the work of Maya Deren. Meshes of the Afternoon [1943] is very original, and very passionate. Even today, I don’t understand what it means, but this is a film that has its own meaning, that gets its message across not through dialogue but through images and emotional involvement. When you see it, you become very affected by it. Of course, there are other wonderful filmmakers like Kurosawa, Fellini, Bertolucci, Hitchcock, but someone I really want to mention is Leni Riefenstahl. NOTEBOOK: Wow. Okay. TSUI: She affected me a lot because I never realized that documentary could be made that way. For a while, I thought I could be a news reporter instead of a dramatic filmmaker. I thought I could be a very good documentary filmmaker. Even today, I think the same. It’s something I really think about. Zu: Warriors from the Magic Mountain (Tsui Hark, 1983). NOTEBOOK: That fusion of Eastern and Western influences was made explicit with Zu: Warriors from the Magic Mountain , which saw you return to the US to hire various special effects teams, including those who worked on Star Wars . The film is often cited as introducing American-style FX to Hong Kong cinema. Tell me about the genesis of this international collaboration. Was it born out of a need to solve a specific set of problems? TSUI: It was really ambitious thinking. The Hong Kong film industry at that time was really restricted by budgetary limitations. You could never think about doing something like Star Wars because you wouldn’t have the money. So, if someone offers the opportunity to try something even close to that, it’s a miracle. And that miracle happened to me. I was given the chance by Mr. Raymond Chow of Golden Harvest. He gave me the opportunity to do anything I wanted to do, probably because he was thinking of different ways in which he could grow his company. But the biggest thanks have to go to someone called Liu Liang-hua. She was behind the whole thing. I don’t know if you’ve heard of her? She was the one who found Bruce Lee in America, and she lent me credibility with Raymond Chow by standing behind me. I took advantage of that opportunity and it opened a door to a different stage in my career. In Hong Kong, in our industry, there was no tradition of post-production work when it came to things like special effects and optical work. It had never been done before in our industry. So I thought that I needed to knock on the doors of people who had done things like that in Hollywood. Hollywood felt like something that you would never be able to get access to. It was somewhere far from my world. But they were extremely friendly. They came over to Hong Kong to help me out with Zu for a period of time. Not for the whole film, but during that time I learned so much. I realized that some things can be done in a certain way, and that other things can be done that have never been done before. That was the inspiration that I got from these people. Zu had a lot of shots to deal with. They came over to help me with some of them, but it wasn’t enough. So I had to discover other ways to solve some problems. I made a lot of mistakes, and those mistakes became jokes in the industry that were passed around. But I thought it was worth it, because you have to go through that process in order to learn from your mistakes. It opened my eyes to how motion pictures could be made in a very silly way—silly, but wonderful. Once Upon a Time in China (Tsui Hark, 1991). NOTEBOOK: You returned to this world in 2001 with The Legend of Zu , this time embracing digital effects technology, which has defined the aesthetic of much of your work since the millennium. Do you relish the freedoms afforded by CGI, or are you nostalgic for the textures and challenges of practical effects? TSUI: I’m frequently asked about artificial intelligence. Do I think of A.I. as an enemy or a friend? I think we’re simply facing a different kind of invention. I remember when I first got a computer, people used to comment that this was the beginning of me cutting myself off from my friends. Then, not long after, everyone started carrying cell phones, and they started looking at those instead of seeing the world. I think whenever we’re faced with something like that, you can’t let it affect your life, or let it affect your whole career. They are not gods. They are not holy spirits that you worship in the temple. You can make use of the technology at that specific time, but it can also backfire because these things are inconsistent and quickly become obsolete. Once everyone has access to the software or the apps, then suddenly everyone can do it. It’s a double-edged sword. I think the most important thing is that you don’t make technology the most important element in making a movie. People talk about cinema being diminished day by day because of the competition from other media. The theatrical business is going down, and maybe cinema will soon be history. But I think the creative side of movies depends too much on a personal vision, not the technical side of things. CGI has nothing to do with someone’s capability to make a good film or not. When you can’t find the money to use these technologies, you find an alternative path to walk. Sometimes, that ends up being a better way. A lot of great films were born that way: the filmmakers couldn’t do something because of a lack of money and conditions, but then they found an alternative solution, leading to a classic that we keep looking at and talking about. NOTEBOOK: Speaking of A.I. and classic films, what did you think of the news last year that the China Film Foundation intends to update Once Upon a Time in China and all the Bruce Lee films using artificial intelligence? TSUI: A.I. can help to make a film look better and get rid of artefacts due to poor preservation. But the most important thing is that you have to keep Bruce Lee clean, and exactly the same as he was before. You cannot change Bruce Lee. One of the problems with A.I. is that it creates something different from what it was in the beginning. You don’t see the reality of the situation any more. That’s one of the big worries with A.I.: its ability to separate you from reality. It can stop you from distinguishing what is real or not. Some people may not care, but it’s scary when you’re not able to tell whether that’s actually Bruce Lee or a computer-generated simulation. But it’s become a very common tool. As soon as A.I. starts replacing creativity, that will be the death of the industry and all manner of professions. Dangerous Encounters: 1st Kind (Tsui Hark, 1980). NOTEBOOK: Zu: Warriors from the Magic Mountain was a critical hit, but it was a real departure from your first three films. Dangerous Encounters: 1st Kind , especially, is a ferocious movie. Where did the rage in that film come from? TSUI: Those feelings were not unique to me. There was a lot of turmoil and instability in the world back then; a lot of questioning of the need to adhere to regulations and traditions. The kind of rage in that film exists in everyone, to different degrees. If you wanted to say something about the world at that time, that was a natural expression. Back then, I was looking for something that could fulfill my urge to make movies. I felt like I was becoming an unexceptional person by making unexceptional movies. So I had to do something about it. I wanted to express my innate personality, making audiences share my compassion and my feelings through provocation. I still feel that way now, it’s just taken on a different means of expression. The films may not be saying “kill everything,” but it’s still in my nature to want to destroy things. If something bothers you a lot, you want to get rid of it. When I look back at Dangerous Encounters now, I think it wants for depth. The anger that I wanted to share with the audience would have lasted longer in their memories if the film had a little more depth. Every time you go to see a movie, you should be getting a new perspective on who you are. That has to be the most important thing about the work you create. You want to reach an audience’s humanity, to trigger an angry or romantic response that helps them remember who they are. We're Going to Eat You (Tsui Hark, 1980). NOTEBOOK: Like your previous film We’re Going to Eat You [1980], it’s a very political picture. How do you think the politics in your filmmaking have evolved over the years? TSUI: We’re Going to Eat You is not a serious film at all. I was just making fun of the horror genre. It’s a comedy! It’s not philosophical and doesn’t have any deep meaning, it was just a parody. When I made that movie, I didn’t really pay much attention to its commercial potential. I was just challenging everyone’s values when it comes to looking at a movie like this. But then I thought, Is this what people are really looking for in their entertainment, or can we give them something else? It was adapted from the work of a famous Chinese writer, and was about people eating people unconsciously. They’re killing each other without knowing why they’re doing it. It was about villagers who cannot accept outsiders, and so they make them disappear. And that’s related to what is happening now. Some people can’t accept that things are changing, they want to keep the old ways. I’m not implying that the new ways are good or bad, but we need to evolve with the times. We need to live with the processes of civilization and humanity, otherwise rigidity and conservatism will destroy our nature. I’m talking about all these things in the movie, but it was totally unserious. NOTEBOOK: Shortly after Zu , you made two of your greatest films in Shanghai Blues and Peking Opera Blues , the first of which recently celebrated its fortieth birthday with a new restoration. Did you watch a lot of screwball comedies while you were studying in the US, and were you thinking of these American films from the 1930s and 1940s while you were developing Shanghai Blues ? TSUI: I’ve always been fond of comedy as a genre. I’m a comedy person. I watched a lot of screwball comedies, Charlie Chaplin, Buster Keaton, Woody Allen, and then a lot of classic Japanese, Chinese, and Indian comedies too. They’re really entertaining. But Shanghai Blues originated from an experience I had with a friend, who told me about a singer who lost her diamond rings on stage. She was meant to start singing, but then looked at her fingers and saw that her rings were missing, so she started screaming instead. I thought that was very funny. I kept thinking about this character, wondering why she was on stage and how she lost her rings. Soon enough, the story came to me when I decided to set it during the Second World War. That period gives us an opportunity to look at human nature in a global sense. Once I had that, the story started to take care of itself: what was this woman’s reality? How did she make her livelihood in this world? That’s the great thing about creativity, it all comes from you. Once you’ve got a trigger that makes everything click into place, it all pours out. And that’s how Shanghai Blues came about. It doesn’t relate to the diamond ring at all, or the scream on the stage, because it turned out I didn’t have room to put that in the film once the story had taken off by itself. There were a lot of problems with that film though, because it was supposed to be a low-budget movie. Which means there are lots of scenes, locations, and special effects that you have to give up. I didn’t have the money to do any special effects properly, so I had to do them very cheaply, using things like papier-mâché and cardboard miniatures. But that was really interesting; that’s real movie-making. You make every effort you can to share an emotion with the audience. The world needs this film’s humour and optimism to make it more lovable and livable. The world is so chaotic, it’s hard to tolerate sometimes. Shanghai Blues (Tsui Hark, 1984). NOTEBOOK: Sylvia Chang and Sally Yeh are wonderful in Shanghai Blues . Do you prefer writing for women? TSUI: I don’t like to think of it that way, because it will limit my ideas. But it’s a bit like baking a cake. You don’t know how it’s going to taste in the end. Sometimes the women’s parts turn out more tasty than the men’s parts. When people tell me I write better parts for women than men, it makes me feel like I need to work harder on my writing for men! NOTEBOOK: Peking Opera Blues is fascinating for its exploration of gender identity, something that recurs throughout your filmography. Tell me a little about your interest in subverting gender roles. TSUI: I know people in real life who are not defined by social criteria. Some people say that a woman should be like this and a man should be like that, but in real life they’re not! And these unique people I know who refuse to be defined that way are usually so interesting. People who don’t follow the rules or go by social standards have a unique attitude towards life, and I respect that. So I don’t think of myself as subverting anything. NOTEBOOK: You had some huge early successes as a producer at Film Workshop, including A Better Tomorrow and A Chinese Ghost Story [1987]. And you’ve earned a reputation over the years for being very hands-on when you’re producing. Tell me a little about your relationships with directors when you have your producing hat on. TSUI: I’ve always been warned about my hands-on habit. I used to think it was a necessity of my role as a producer, but I’ve tried to get out of the … what should I call it? … the curse of my habit. I never know if I’m a good producer or not, because sometimes the projects turn out wonderful, and other times I worry that I’ve ruined them. But I do know what I’m talking about. If there’s something I shouldn’t have put my hands on, I try to get out of it. And then if I think I need to do something about something, then I’ll just do it. It’s often hard to distinguish which films I should get fully involved with and which ones I shouldn’t. A Better Tomorrow III: Love and Death in Saigon (Tsui Hark, 1989). NOTEBOOK: You produced some incredible films for John Woo, before parting company on A Better Tomorrow III: Love and Death in Saigon , which you directed yourself. Woo later directed his own variation with Bullet in the Head [1990]. It seems there are a lot of conflicting stories out there about the reasons for your split, so I was wondering if you could talk a little about what happened there? TSUI: That incident involved a lot of outside factors. John Woo had gained a great reputation by that point, and he was able to start looking at going into international productions. Meanwhile, I was hugely entangled in an industry production crisis that was going on at the time. He didn’t want to do episode three. He thought we’d already reached the end of it. And I agreed! But I was tempted to say more about this character by looking into his past. Then I realized I was wrong. It didn’t turn out how I expected. John Woo had his own way of developing films with his company, and that’s why we separated, in a way. Of course, there’s no separation without negative feelings. That came about from him thinking I was interfering with his creativity, that sort of thing. And I agree that I did have that problem. It happens to everybody in the creative industries, though. You never know whose idea is right or wrong until you see the results. A few years later we got together and talked about it, and we felt that this was now in the past and there were more important things coming up. Later, I tried to do as much as possible in terms of helping him out. We managed to maintain our friendship, and we both feel, just like the characters in A Better Tomorrow , that we’re brothers. NOTEBOOK: From the climaxes of Peking Opera Blues and Once Upon a Time in China , to The Blade and Time and Tide —you’ve directed some breathtaking action set pieces. Tell me a little about how you put these sequences together? Do you storyboard? TSUI: I only use storyboards when I have to use special effects, because people need to know the budget and what the technical requirements will be. Otherwise the producer, or the person in charge of the money, gets very insecure. You need storyboards when you want to create a spectacular shot. I always tell the storyboard artists that spectacular doesn’t mean there needs to be thousands of people, spectacular can just mean a close-up of an eye. A pupil alone can do a lot of things to an audience’s response. There have been different trends in action throughout the history of the genre. Every new trend creates a new sensation, and this has to be designed by necessity. Each one creates a new reality. Look at Jurassic Park [1993]. Suddenly dinosaurs are in front of you, walking around. You want to create difficult action that has never been seen before in the movies. Like when Jackie Chan slides down thirty floors from the roof of a building. You’d never seen that before. The romantic element of the action itself is also hugely important. People want to be a part of the action, and there’s often a romantic element that causes people to love the scene. But everything always changes, according to the times, because so many people try to imitate the new trend. And then it dies off because people have seen too much of it. You have to try to start a new trend with every movie, because you’re always going to face the same problem. The Blade (Tsui Hark, 1995). NOTEBOOK: The Blade is an astonishing action picture, and a film that seems to resurrect the nihilistic energy of Dangerous Encounter . It’s your spin on Chang Cheh’s The One-Armed Swordsman [1967], but it’s a film that seems set on tearing down and brutalizing what came before. How did the project evolve into something so dark and subversive? TSUI: It’s interesting because I wanted to go back to a documentary style. It wasn’t easy, because so much relies on your cameraman being able to catch a sudden moment. It’s like being in the middle of a riot, where everything relies on capturing the moment a policeman strikes a demonstrator amid the chaos. It’s not something you can plan. But with a movie, you have to plan, because otherwise the performers won’t know what’s going on, and that’s dangerous. So you have to imitate unplannedness while actually planning very carefully. We ended up using an intercom system where I was talking to the cameraman, telling him to catch a glimpse of the guy emerging from the alley swinging his sword, before quickly panning away to the girl running away. It was chaotic, but fun. I didn’t have any dialogue in the script. The dialogue was invented by the performers, and that was very challenging for them. They had to figure out what to say, which was particularly hard in the emotional scenes. It was an experiment, and other people have tried it since, with very good results. NOTEBOOK: Your films have been getting bigger and bigger in recent years. Do you have any desire to make smaller-scale pictures like those from earlier in your career? TSUI: I always want to go back to low-budget filmmaking. The industry is shrinking and it’s a dire situation. People are suffering. The theatrical business is sinking, with competition coming from other media. The cinema circuit is shrinking. It’s a very bad time for movies. But nothing is worse than war. War is a terrible thing to happen to human beings. Although I do think that creativity always emerges after suffering.

Claude Opus 4.8: "a modest but tangible improvement"

Simon Willison's Weblog · 2026-06-09 06:38 UTC

Anthropic shipped Claude Opus 4.8 today. My favourite thing about it is this note in the release announcement: Users will find Opus 4.8 to be a modest but tangible improvement on its predecessor. There’s still more to be done: we’re working on developing and releasing models that provide many of the same capabilities as Opus at a lower cost. It's so refreshing to see an AI lab honestly describe a release as a minor incremental improvement over the previous model! Honesty seems to be a theme. Here's my other favorite note from that announcement: One of the most prominent improvements in Opus 4.8 is its honesty . We train all our models to be honest---for instance, to avoid making claims that they can't support. But a general problem with AI models is that they sometimes jump to conclusions, confidently claiming to have made progress in their work despite the evidence being thin. Early testers report that Opus 4.8 is more likely to flag uncertainties about its work and less likely to make unsupported claims. This is borne out in our evaluations , which show that Opus 4.8 is around four times less likely than its predecessor to allow flaws in code it has written to pass unremarked. That linked system card includes the following: Claude Opus 4.8 had the lowest incorrect-rate of the six models on every benchmark—the most direct measure of factual hallucination. It achieved this mainly by abstaining on questions about which it was uncertain rather than by answering more questions correctly. Model characteristics Not much has changed since 4.7. It's priced the same as Opus 4.5/4.6/4.7 - $5/million input and $25 per million output. "Fast mode" is twice that price, which is a significant reduction from their previous models - fast mode on 4.6/4.7 remains at $30/$150. Note that fast mode is only available to organizations that are part of the research preview, "Contact your account manager to request access". Both the reliable knowledge cutoff and the training data cutoff are January 2026, the same as for 4.7. The context window is still 1,000,000 tokens, and the max output is 128,000 tokens. The What's new in Claude Opus 4.8 document has some of the more interesting details. These caught my eye: Mid-conversation system messages . Claude Opus 4.8 accepts role: "system" messages immediately after a user turn in the messages array (subject to placement rules ). This lets you append updated instructions later in a long-running conversation without restating the full system prompt, which preserves prompt cache hits on the earlier turns and reduces input cost on agentic loops. See also this update to the Anthropic Python SDK. Being able to steer the system prompt mid-conversation sounds really powerful. I was worried this would be incompatible with the abstraction provided by my own LLM library , which expects a single system prompt per conversation... but it turns out my recent redesign should handle that just fine . Lower prompt cache minimum . The minimum cacheable prompt length on Claude Opus 4.8 is 1,024 tokens, lower than on Claude Opus 4.7. I checked and 4.7's minimum was 4,096 . Internet Age Gates Are a Growing Global Threat

Electronic Frontier Foundation · 2026-06-05 19:28 UTC

The internet is an essential resource for young people and adults to access information, explore community, and find themselves—both inside countries and across continents. Yet governments around the world continue to introduce and implement legislation requiring all online users to verify their ages before accessing the digital space. In some cases, politicians are going further, putting forth proposals to ban social media for younger users. In late 2025, Australia’s government rolled out the first complete ban on users under 16 from having social media accounts. In this sweeping regime, platforms are required to introduce age assurance tools to block under-16s, demonstrate that they have taken “reasonable steps” to deactivate accounts used by under-16s, and prevent any new accounts being created, or face fines of up to 49.5 million Australian dollars ($32 million USD). The 10 banned platforms—Instagram, Facebook, Threads, Snapchat, YouTube, TikTok, Kick, Reddit, Twitch, and X—have each said they’ll comply with the legislation, which led to young people losing access to their accounts overnight . Reddit is currently challenging the law in Australian courts on constitutional grounds. Recent research notes how the ban is preventing teenagers from accessing news in the country. In the United Kingdom , rules took effect in mid-2025 under the Online Safety Act that require all online services available in the country to assess whether they host content considered harmful to children; if so, these services must introduce age checks to prevent children from accessing such content. Online services are also required to change their algorithms and moderation systems to ensure that content defined as harmful, like violent imagery, is not shown to young people. This approach is reckless, short-sighted, and we’ve already seen it introduce more harm to the young people that it is trying to protect. The UK’s scramble to find an effective age verification method shows us that there isn't one, and we’ve spent years urging UK politicians to abandon any measures that require platforms to collect data or remove privacy protections around users’ identities. Earlier this year, Indonesia’s Communications and Digital Affairs Minister, Meutya Hafid, announced that users under 16 would have their accounts on “high risk” platforms deactivated from 28 March. The platforms subject to this ban are YouTube, TikTok, Facebook, Instagram, Threads, X, Bigo Live, and Roblox; with Hafid noting how this policy would make Indonesia “the first non-Western country to delay children's access to digital spaces according to age.” Similarly, the Malaysian government has recently pushed forward with plans to ban users under 16 from having accounts on social media platforms with at least 8 million users in Malaysia, including Facebook, Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube. Users under the age of 16 are being told to download or transfer their data from these platforms in one month before the restrictions are applied. Platforms failing to comply with the ban may face penalties of up to $2.5 million USD. In Latin America, Brazil approved a new law in 2025 establishing that providers of information technology products and services directed to children and teenagers, or likely to be accessed by them, must conduct age checks when their products and services offer risks to underage users. Regulation requires age assurance for products and services that are not allowed for children and adolescents in accordance with Brazilian legislation. App stores and operating systems are required to provide age signals for other providers. While the law is already in force, full compliance with its obligations is expected for early 2027 , after the approval of further regulations and a transition period, and the authority responsible for enforcing the law is the Brazilian National Data Protection Agency . The list of concerns regarding the implementation of the law include: the wide scope of products and services that may fall within age-check obligations, how these obligations can affect non-proprietary operating systems and free software projects, and how effective the law's crucial data protection safeguards will be in a context of likely widespread age checks for accessing content online. Similarly, the European Union has taken large steps towards mandatory age verification that could undermine privacy, expression, and participation rights for everyone. Politicians are promoting an EU-wide approach to age verification through its age verification “app,” which will be fully interoperable with the Digital Identity Wallet . While this mini-app has been announced as technically ready to be rolled out “for citizens to use,” it comes with its own realm of potential privacy and security concerns , such as long-term identifiers (which could result in tracking) and over-exposure of personal information. The European Commission also supports age verification in various legislative initiatives, from proposals that would allow or mandate companies to scan our communication (“ Chat Control ”) to non-binding guidelines of existing laws, such as the Digital Services Act. The EU Parliament, too, has proposed an EU digital minimum age of 16 for access to social media, a move that aligns with EU Commission’s president Ursula von der Leyen’s recent public support for measures inspired by Australia’s model. To all these initiatives EFF has provided one consistent response: mandatory age verification measures are not the right way to protect young people. These proposals restrict the fundamental rights of young people to speak to each other and to access information. They also force all internet users, not just those under a certain age, to upload private data—like a face scan or passport—in order to access a website or service. In considering the vast scope of privacy issues pertaining to the collection, storage, and sharing of this personal information, the problems of age verification in restricting free speech are compounded by these reckless and harmful approaches to verification. The problem of censorship and surveillance goes far beyond the borders of the internet. EFF continues to explore support for legislative and litigation challenges that recognize how these laws harm everyone’s rights to privacy, free expression and due process.

Rediscovering the Handcart

Low-Tech Magazine · 2026-04-22 00:00 UTC

Image: The handcart, equipped with a sail. Photo by Kris De Decker. The human-powered handcart is the oldest of vehicles, and it will likely be the last one around in the future. Of all vehicles, it&rsquo;s the cheapest and least complex to build and use. It offers a large advantage over carrying a load on your back or dragging it over the ground - the even older concept of the sled. On the other hand, the handcart is cheaper and easier to use than the animal-powered cart. Oxen and donkeys eat more than humans, and they have their own will, which can work against the driver. Like any other wheeled vehicle, the handcart requires roads to drive on. This infrastructure has not always been available anywhere or at any time in history. For example, in medieval Europe, porters and pack animals were more common than handcarts because of poor roads. 1 In the West, the handcart only reached its heyday during the first decades of the Industrial Revolution, when it connected fast-growing cities to train stations and harbors. In China, on the other hand, the handcart was the backbone of the transport network for millennia. 2 Of all vehicles, the handcart is the cheapest and least complex to build and use. There are still many human-powered carts in modern society: strollers, grocery carts, roller suitcases, and various utility and folding carts. However, these modern carts are to their predecessors what birds are to dinosaurs. They are small, often with very small wheels, and we use them for very short distances, usually inside buildings. In contrast, old-fashioned handcarts were often large and had big wheels, and they were pushed or pulled on roads and over longer distances. Many crafts and professions had their own type of handcart. Image: Low-tech Magazine's handcart. Photo by Kris De Decker. Why I need a handcart People still use large handcarts in so-called &ldquo;developing countries&rdquo;. However, they can be just as useful again in the large cities of the industrialized world, as I can testify after using one for a couple of months. Last autumn, I received an internship application from Kozimo, who studies at the Design Academy Eindhoven. In his application, Kozimo sent a video of a large handcart he made, which he was driving on the streets of Rotterdam, the Netherlands. I have always dreamt of a handcart. I have never owned a car, and the only times I miss one are when I have to move stuff, something which has become increasingly common lately. Consequently, I proposed to Kozimo to build a handcart for me. Now, I can no longer imagine living without it. I have used the vehicle to move houses and offices, pick up materials and objects I bought online, new or second-hand, and transport workshop and event materials (bike generators, solar panels, solar ovens, books, sound systems). I have done the same for friends. During these trips, I often took home materials, furniture, or objects that I found for free on the streets of Barcelona. Image: Kozimo and Kris De Decker with Low-tech Magazine's handcart, halfway through a 30 km trip along the coast of Spain. Photo by Linda Osusky. Unlike a van or a car, my handcart doesn&rsquo;t need gasoline, electricity, or batteries, making it entirely independent from energy infrastructures. Neither do I need to pay taxes and insurance. The handcart is a very democratic vehicle. It allows anyone to carry a load wherever they want, while older, less affordable cars and vans are no longer allowed to enter city centers due to the installation of Low Emission Zones. A handcart doesn&rsquo;t need gasoline, electricity, or batteries, making it entirely independent from energy infrastructures. It would make a lot of sense to offer vehicles like this at community centers, where they are available for all neighbors to use when needed. Few people would need a handcart each day, and communal use would solve the parking problem. Although our handcart can also be parked vertically, it won&rsquo;t fit in most apartments. Description of the handcart This article will not explain in detail how to build a handcart. We want to do that another time with a simpler handcart model, because the vehicle we present in this article is not one that most people can make themselves. You need good woodworking and metalworking skills, and in fact, two people made the handcart. Kozimo designed and built the whole structure from wood, while Guilhem Senges - visual artist and one of my neighbors - designed and made several essential reinforcements from metal; the wheels, the brakes, and the handlebars are all connected to the wood structure with custom-made iron parts. Load weight and volume Low-tech Magazine&rsquo;s handcart is 250 cm long and 100 cm wide, while the platform itself measures 210 by 85 cm. Assuming a load height of 50 cm, the cargo volume is roughly 1.55 m3 (37 cubic feet or 1050 liters). That&rsquo;s two to four times the typical trunk space in a European car. We have transported cargo that is wider or longer than the cart: a large heated table measuring 140x140cm, and several loads of wooden beams, each three meters long. The load weight is limited by the wheels, which come from a wheelchair. They can support up to 150 kg. 3 The cart itself weighs 32 kg, so the practical maximum cargo weight is about 120 kg. The loading platform consists of slats with gaps between them, making it easy to secure various types of cargo. Images: The handcart with various cargoes. Upper left: a 6m2 wooden floor and a chest. Upper right: 3-meter-long wood beams. Below: A heated table ready for transport. It drives itself! Over the past few months, we&rsquo;ve learned that people have many misconceptions about handcarts. For example, you may think that pushing a handcart takes a lot of effort, perhaps based on your experience pushing supermarket carts through parking lots or pulling heavy suitcases through city centers (which is how I moved stuff before I had a handcart). However, using the handcart can be so effortless - even when it&rsquo;s heavily loaded - that it feels like you are not pushing at all. Once in motion, you can often guide it with one hand, and it sometimes feels like the cart is pulling you forward. It&rsquo;s no exaggeration to say that pushing the handcart with a 100 kg load is more comfortable than walking while carrying a 10 kg heavy backpack. Using the handcart can be so effortless - even when it&rsquo;s heavily loaded - that it feels like you are not pushing at all. There are several reasons for this light operation, rooted in physics. Each vehicle has to overcome three forces: rolling resistance, air resistance, and gravity. Air resistance is negligible at walking speed, meaning that a handcart user on flat terrain mainly needs to overcome rolling resistance. That&rsquo;s the friction between wheels and road surface, a factor that&rsquo;s largely independent of speed. In contrast, air resistance increases with the square of speed. A cyclist, going at 15-20 km/h, already spends more effort overcoming air resistance than overcoming rolling resistance, which is the same in both cases because both vehicles have similar wheels. In short, the handcart&rsquo;s low speed minimizes air resistance, while its narrow wheels minimize rolling resistance. Image: Driving the handcart. Photo by Linda Osusky. Second, accelerating a vehicle requires more energy than maintaining a constant speed. You only need to sustain momentum, not build it. Our handcart is pushed by a person walking, so the effort to accelerate lasts no longer than one or two seconds. In contrast, a cyclist takes much longer to reach cruise speed, and because of the higher air resistance, it takes more effort to sustain that speed. If the handcart is heavily loaded, it also gains significant kinetic energy, even at low speed. That explains why it sometimes feels like the cart is pulling you forward - because it actually is. Finally, our wheels are much larger than those used on modern pushcarts. That makes for comfortable driving on asphalt and sidewalks, which are not as smooth as airport or supermarket floors. Large wheels increase air resistance, but because of our low speed, that doesn&rsquo;t matter. Handcarts and gravity However, an effortless ride requires two conditions: flat terrain and a well-balanced load. Both involve the third force any vehicle must overcome: gravity. Balancing the handcart: distributing the load A two-wheeled cart becomes heavy and difficult to use when too much weight is placed on the front or back. Consequently, you need to load the vehicle so that the weight is equal on both sides of the wheels. That&rsquo;s easy to check: the cart should remain in a horizontal position for several seconds without you touching it. If there&rsquo;s just one piece of cargo, place it above the center of the wheels. If there are more things to carry, the total weight should be divided equally over the two sides. Finetuning the balance often involves moving a backpack from the front to the back of the cart, or vice versa. You need to load the vehicle so that the weight is equal on both sides of the wheels. A two-wheeled cart also needs additional support to keep it horizontal when parked, for instance, when loading or unloading cargo. Otherwise, the cart may suddenly flip to the other side. Our handcart carries four support beams, two on each side. When the cart is moving, they are in a horizontal position. When the cart is parked, we remove one or more beams and place them in a vertical position. Each beam can be set to a different length, allowing us to stabilize the cart on uneven terrain. We tighten the beams with screws. Image: The handcart is parked with four supporting legs. Photo by Kris De Decker. Image: Detail of the supporting beam holder. Photo by Kris De Decker. Many people have asked us why we didn&rsquo;t build a four-wheeled cart that wouldn&rsquo;t need to be balanced. However, four wheels would double the rolling resistance and thus the effort required to push the cart. Furthermore, a four-wheeled cart is less maneuverable and more difficult to drive on uneven terrain. You also need to get two extra wheels, and you need to build a steering mechanism. Throughout history, the two-wheeled handcart (or one-wheeled handcart in the case of China) was much more common than the four-wheeled cart. 1 Going uphill: you need help An effortless ride also requires more or less flat terrain, which is what you get here in many parts of Barcelona. If you go up a steep slope, you suddenly feel the weight of the cart and its cargo. Climbing with a heavily loaded cart can be as strenuous as running up stairs or cycling at top speed. People tell us we should put an electric motor on the cart, and that&rsquo;s perfectly possible. However, we found a simpler solution: if necessary, we ask for help from another person. Our handlebars are wide enough for two or even three people to push together, which makes going uphill a lot easier. Adding an electric motor and a battery would significantly increase the vehicle&rsquo;s weight, and it only makes sense if you regularly have to climb hills. Going downhill: brakes Going downhill, you have to counter gravity forces to prevent the handcart from hurling down a slope, which would be very dangerous. Rather than pushing the cart, you&rsquo;ll have to pull it back instead. Here, cyclists have all the advantage, as they can use gravity to its full benefit during a descent. We made going downhill a lot easier by adding bicycle brakes. In combination with the large wheels, the brakes also allow the handcart to be taken down sidewalk curbs or even stairs without damaging it. They double as a hand brake as well, by tightening two lashing straps around them. That allows leaving the cart unattended on a slope or in high winds. Image: The brakes. Photo by Kris De Decker. Handcarts go on the sidewalk Many people assume that handcarts go on the road, with the cars, or on the cycling path. That&rsquo;s not the case: you use it on the sidewalk. Legally, handcart users are in a similar position to other pedestrians pushing a smaller handcart, such as a stroller. The only difference is that, when they are forced onto the road because there&rsquo;s no sidewalk or it&rsquo;s blocked, handcart users should walk on the right side of the road, while other pedestrians should walk on the left. For now, the police have stopped us only once, and they were just curious. Legally, handcart users are in a similar position to other pedestrians pushing a smaller handcart, such as a stroller. We could find no traffic laws that limit the size of a handcart, at least not in the handful of countries we researched, including Spain. However, in practice, there are clear limits. If your vehicle is wider than the space between traffic bollards that keep cars out of pedestrian streets, all pedestrian zones will become inaccessible to you. You should also take into account other obstacles on the sidewalk, such as building scaffolding. Consequently, it&rsquo;s rarely practical to build a handcart more than one meter wide. Barcelona has very wide sidewalks in most of the city. We rarely have to share the road with cars or cyclists. Of course, that&rsquo;s not the case in every city, and then the use of a handcart becomes less attractive. Using a handcart on the road or cyclepath is rather dangerous because other vehicles are much faster. Image: Kozimo pushes the handcart through a narrow walkway. Photo by Kris De Decker. Respecting other pedestrians Driving a large handcart on the sidewalk demands your full attention. You don&rsquo;t want to hit any infrastructure, and you surely don&rsquo;t want to hit someone&rsquo;s legs. You need to drive it with respect for other pedestrians and their pets (some dogs start barking at the vehicle). In general, the handcart is very safe to use because it travels at a very low speed. That makes accidents less likely in the first place and less impactful if they do happen. You also have a very good overview of your vehicle, much better than for a car or a bicycle. As long as you keep your eyes on the handcart, you are unlikely to hit anything or anyone. However, our handcart is so silent that people don&rsquo;t hear it coming. We added a bicycle bell to warn people, but we hope to find a better tune in the future: every vehicle needs its own type of sound. We also need a bell for oncoming pedestrians who are watching their phones while walking and expect others to make space. With the handcart, we cannot always make that space. Our handcart has front and rear lights as well, wired to a USB power bank mounted underneath the platform. Lights are very helpful on sidewalks, both day and night, as they make the vehicle more visible. Furthermore, lights are essential if you need to move onto the road after dark. Images: Kris De Decker drives the handcart through Barcelona. Photos by Guillaume Lion. Even in Barcelona, sidewalks can get crowded, and a busy sidewalk will slow down the vehicle considerably. With little chance to overtake someone, we tend to get stuck behind the slowest walkers. A handcart is not a difficult vehicle to drive, but nowadays people in industrialized societies have no experience with it. Apart from driving it attentively, you also need to be careful when rounding blind corners (take the turn as wide as possible) and when you leave a garage or any other type of exit (pull rather than push the cart). By the time you see oncoming traffic, you already have 2 meters of your handcart on the road or around the corner. Why not a bike trailer? Almost everyone who sees the handcart for the first time asks the same question: how do you attach it to a bicycle? You don&rsquo;t. You push it while walking. When we say that, there follows a silence. Pushing a handcart seems like one step too far back, even for people committed to living more sustainably. Why would you push a handcart if you could just as well use a much faster bike trailer, or a cargo bike? In fact, there are several practical reasons to opt for a handcart rather than a bike trailer, and we have already mentioned many of them. First, a handcart lets you go anywhere a pedestrian can, while cyclists often need to get off their bikes and push them - just like a handcart. A handcart is also more agile. For example, although the cart is 2.5 meters long, it takes just two seconds and little space to turn it around and walk in the opposite direction from where you came from. Why would you push a handcart if you could just as well use a much faster bike trailer, or a cargo bike? A handcart can be built larger than a bike trailer as well. Although it&rsquo;s perfectly possible to build a bike trailer the size of our handcart, its higher speed would pose much greater risk of accidents and damage, both to the cart and to other road users. As a bike trailer, it would also need to be made sturdier, and it would need a more elaborate mechanism to operate the brakes. All this does not mean that bike trailers are a bad idea. We have used the handcart mainly for trips between 5 and 10 km, which comes down to one to two hours of walking. For longer distances, the bike trailer has the obvious advantage of speed. If you need to cover 40 km, you would need to travel eight hours with a handcart, compared to just two hours with a bike trailer. Image: Guilhem Senges, who built the vehicle's metal parts, pushes the handcart to a welding job a few streets up in the neighborhood. The merits of slow travel However, when people ask us why we don&rsquo;t use it as a bike trailer, we can also answer differently: why the rush? Deciding to travel with the slowest vehicle possible is subversive because it questions values we take for granted in the modern world, such as speed and utility. To many people, walking a handcart seems like a waste of time, but our experience is exactly the opposite. Every trip is an adventure, and we always look forward to using it again. It&rsquo;s a pleasure to drive the vehicle, more like steering a boat than driving a land vehicle. It&rsquo;s easy to chat with other pedestrians, who tend to be very curious about our vehicle. Consequently, the trip takes even longer. To many people, walking a handcart seems like a waste of time, but our experience is exactly the opposite. Driving a handcart feels entirely different from using any other mode of transport. When people are walking, they usually cannot carry much with them, either in terms of weight or volume. In contrast, the handcart allows you to walk with a lot of stuff close at hand: drinks, food, a sound system, books, extra clothes. Furthermore, you have a large platform, which allows you to rest and invite others to do the same. It becomes a vehicle for wandering and roaming, and for connecting to other people. Photo by Kris De Decker. Handcart Accessories Once the handcart proved its utility as a cargo vehicle, Kozimo began designing and building additional structures to expand its uses. These objects make use of the slatted platform or the support beam design. Unfortunately, Kozimo&rsquo;s internship ended before we could test all these extensions, but the little experience we gained by now shows that the handcart can be much more than just a cargo vehicle. Passenger seat The first, and perhaps most powerful addition, is a foldable seat. While our handcart can be - and usually is - operated by only one person, it&rsquo;s ideally handled by two people, especially for longer voyages. Thanks to the seat, one person can push the cart while the other one rests in the vehicle. As long as the road is flat, the extra weight of the passenger does not significantly increase the effort to push the cart. Consequently, two people can travel faster or farther in a single day. When climbing hills or bridges, the passenger gets off the seat. If necessary, he or she also helps to push the cart. One person can push the cart while the other one rests in the vehicle, increasing the distance that two people can travel in a day. An extra pair of eyes on the road is also handy. The seat can be put in two positions, so that both the passenger and the driver are either looking in the same direction or facing each other, which makes it easier to talk and allows the passenger to serve as the rear-view mirror. We used the seat on a 30 km day trip along the coast of Catalunya, Spain, moving stuff from my old place to my new place. For one person, this would have been an exhausting trip. However, there were several people on the way there, and two people on the way back. The fact that we could rest from time to time - without stopping - made a great difference, especially on the way back. An extra person also proved useful when unexpected obstacles arose. For example, there was a bridge under repair, which forced us to carry the cart down the rocks, over the beach, and up the rocks again. Image: A foldable seat on the slatted platform. If you&rsquo;re alone, you can wheel the vehicle to the nearest park or beach, set up the four support legs, and work all day. Renewable power plant Although we built only one solar panel support structure, the handcart platform is large enough to support a total of four 100-watt solar panels. That would provide us with 400 watts of solar power for a concert or emergency power, for example. The handcart can also transport the two bike generators Low-tech Magazine has in Barcelona. 5 Consequently, the cart enables us to quickly provide power within a radius of several kilometers, at any time of the day. The handcart could also be wheeled into a sunny spot during the day, charging a battery bank to power a household during the night and in bad weather. Mobile home If you want to get back home the same day, the handcart&rsquo;s range is roughly 40-80 km (8-16 hours of walking, back and forth). However, at least in my case, nobody obliges me to come back home the same day. I could use the handcart for longer voyages, especially since it offers me a place to sleep. The four supporting legs that make loading and unloading the cart more practical can also be used to turn the vehicle into a bed. After Kozimo went back to the Netherlands, I bought a foldable mattress that fits neatly on the platform. During a trip, I can store the other cargo under the cart at night. Alternatively, I could push a passenger who&rsquo;s lying in the bed, turning the vehicle into an adult version of a baby stroller. Images: A foldable sleeping mattress on the handcart. Furthermore, I could use the supporting legs in various combinations to convert the cart into a podium, expo stand, market stand, or a cinema or presentation screen. The seat, table, solar panel, sleeping mattress, and longer poles can all be carried on the handcart simultaneously, leaving ample space for other luggage. That means that I could potentially work, live, and travel in the vehicle, turning it into a nomadic home. It fits somewhere between the tiny house on wheels, the tipi, and the homeless shack. Sailing and roller skating the handcart Finally, Kozimo made a small sail for the handcart to help pull a heavy load in a good wind; the vehicle is sometimes used along the coast. Of course, we got the inspiration from the use of sails on the historical Chinese wheelbarrow . For a longer trip, the sail fits on the cart, so I could use it whenever the opportunity arises. We could increase the speed of the handcart by using a larger sail, and combining it with roller blades, inline skates, or a skateboard. In that case, the cart would pull the driver in good winds. It&rsquo;s also possible to push the cart while using roller blades, inline skates, or an electric unicycle, without a sail. For now, we did a first small test on flat terrain using inline skates, with very good results. Winter is Coming: Build a Solar Powered Foot Stove

Low-Tech Magazine · 2025-11-16 00:00 UTC

Image: The electric foot stove that we build in this manual. Photo by Marie Verdeil. We built an electric cube heater, powered by a 100-watt solar PV panel. During the day, the solar panel slowly heats the cube, which radiates heat to its surroundings. Due to its high thermal mass, the object continues to radiate heat for hours after sunset. Electric Foot Stove The heat cube can serve multiple purposes. You can use it as a modern variant of a preindustrial foot stove . Put your feet on the cube and throw a blanket over your lap to trap the heat. Historically, foot stoves contained glowing sintels from the fireplace, but an electric version is safer and healthier. There is no risk for carbon monoxide poisoning or fire. The heat cube contains no flammable materials. Image: A nineteenth century foot stove. Source: Museum Rotterdam (CC BY-SA 3.0). You can also put the cube under a table that has a blanket on top, and that is another method to build an electrically heated table . The heat cube can also quickly dry a pair of socks or keep a prepared dish warm in the kitchen or on the table. Rather than storing electricity from a solar panel in a battery to operate an electric heater at night, the solar panel stores heat in the thermal mass of the heat cube itself. That&rsquo;s cheaper and more sustainable, because batteries account for 70-90% of the money and energy invested in an off-grid solar PV system. Heat can be stored for even later in the night by covering the cube with one or more wool blankets . Image: The heat cube used as a hand and foot warmer. Photo by Marie Verdeil. Bricks, Mortar, and Tiles We created the heat cube using inexpensive and simple materials: bricks, mortar, and tiles. The solar panel supplies power to an electric resistance heater, which we made ourselves and which we laid between several layers of bricks. The electric resistance heater connects directly to the solar panel, without a solar charge controller or voltage regulator in between. If you add more solar panels, the heat cube also works in cloudy weather. Electric Tile Stove Our heat cube is relatively small (20x20x25cm), but this manual can serve to build a much larger version, which could take the form of an electric tile stove , which could include a heated bench or sleeping platform. Tile stoves date back to the Middle Ages and accumulate heat from a biomass fire within a stone or brick mass. They are fired only once or twice per day and continue to radiate heat for approximately 12 to 24 hours. However, a tile stove can also work electrically. In that case, there is no need to add a labyrinth of smoke channels that delays the release of heat through the chimney; instead, only layers of bricks with electric heating elements in between are required. Neither do you need a chimney. Image: A 1920 tile stove in the "Tautes Heim". Source: www.tautshome.com (CC BY 3.0). Tile stoves are very heavy, and powering them electrically doesn&rsquo;t make that any better. Once built, they remain in place. Even our small cube heater is not precisely a portable device: it weighs 26 kg. If you want to move it around, it&rsquo;s a good idea to put it on a wooden board with wheels mounted underneath. Make sure it can&rsquo;t slide down a slope. Solar Oven vs. Foot Stove This solar-powered cube heater forms part of a larger collection of devices that we built, including a solar powered oven and a solar powered coffee maker . They are all based on direct solar power and heat storage rather than electric energy storage. All these devices are inspired by tile stoves. However, while the solar oven and coffee maker are designed to keep the heat inside, the cube heater is designed to radiate heat outwards. Therefore, it is the only appliance that has no thermal insulation. That also makes it easier, quicker, and cheaper to build than the others. Thermostat The heat cube we built works without a thermostat. Because it has no thermal insulation, there is little danger of overheating and damage to the electric resistance circuit. It&rsquo;s the room that gets warmer: the cube continues to radiate heat into its environment and maintains a steady temperature. Furthermore, the sun goes down every evening, cutting off the power supply. However, overheating could occur when you charge the heater with one or more blankets on top, effectively adding insulation, or when you operate it on grid power using an AC/DC converter. To avoid this, you can add a thermostat, which turns off the power source when the surface temperature exceeds a preset limit. A thermostat can also be helpful when the heat cube surface becomes too hot to touch with bare skin. However, you can also wrap a blanket around it or make a fitting cover, similar to the ones used for hot water bottles . Installing an on/off button provides you with a manually operated thermostat. What You Need Six bricks. We used refractory bricks (also known as fire bricks), which are used in high-temperature environments. Refractory bricks are made of silica and store thermal energy very well. However, regular bricks work as well, because they withstand temperatures much higher than those reached in the cube heater. Just make sure the bricks have a 1:2 size ratio, allowing you to create a pile with alternating brick layers. Mortar. We used regular construction mortar, which can withstand temperatures up to 300°C. Refractory cement combined with sand and water could also work. However, it&rsquo;s not a requirement because the heater doesn&rsquo;t reach a temperature of 300°C. Electric resistance heating element. We made a 100-watt electric resistance heater, which we fixed in the mortar between the bricks. It&rsquo;s made from nichrome wire and heat-resistant electric cables. See our separate manual . Tiles. We used ceramic tiles to cover the sides and the top of the cube, and a thicker floor tile at the base. The tiles radiate heat, waterproof the structure, and help to keep it together. Adhesive mortar. To paste the tiles to the bricks. A fuse. You add this in the positive wire between the solar panel and the heat cube. Its Amps rating should be slightly higher than that of the heating element. Read more about fuses . A thermostat (optional). In contrast to the other devices in this collection, the heat cube does not have a thermal switch and thermal fuse. You could add a thermostat to regulate the temperature of the heat cube (see our manual on building an electrically heated table ). An on/off switch (optional). Add this when you don&rsquo;t have a thermostat. Image: Step by step instructions to build the heat cube. Illustration by Marie Verdeil. Building steps Cut a large and thick floor tile into a square. Its dimensions should be bigger than two bricks lying side by side. We have more information about cutting tiles in the solar oven manual . Prepare the heat-resistant circuit of 100W. Since we have three layers of bricks, we prepare two separate strands of nichrome wire to evenly spread the heat. One strand goes between the first and second layers of bricks, and the other between the second and third layers. Solder both strands to heat-resistant electric cables. Next, connect these heat-resistant electric cables in parallel. To decide the length and the thickness of the nichrome wire, and other building steps, consult our separate manual . Prepare some construction mortar following the package instructions. Apply some mortar to the bottom tile and place your first brick on top of it. Add some mortar on the inner side of the second brick before pressing it next to the first brick. Add a generous amount of mortar on top of this first level and place the first part of the resistance circuit, ensuring the nichrome wires are spread evenly, don&rsquo;t cross, and remain in the center. Take care that the nichrome wire doesn&rsquo;t come closer than 5 cm to the sides of the cube, otherwise the surface becomes too hot. Cover with more mortar and layer a second level of two bricks, perpendicular to the first one. Place some mortar on top of the second level before laying down the second part of the circuit. Make sure the ends of both circuits are on the same side of the cube and that the heat-resistant cable ends extend at least 5 cm beyond the cube. We will connect them in parallel later. Place the last layer of bricks, perpendicular to the second level. Use the leftover mortar to fill the holes on the edges of the bricks. Wait 24 hours for the mortar to cure. Solder the two separate circuits together in parallel, ensuring there are a few centimeters of space left to extend the circuit with regular cables later. Prepare some adhesive mortar and cut the tiles for the five sides of the heating cube (except the bottom). Drill two holes for the cables through the tile on the side where they stick out. Apply generous amounts of adhesive mortar on all sides and cover the surface. Let it dry according to the mixing instructions. Once dry, apply grout in between the tiles&rsquo; edges. Connect regular electric cables to each strand to create a power supply cable. We made it 1m long. Optionally add connectors at the end of each cable. The simple resistance circuit has no polarity.
