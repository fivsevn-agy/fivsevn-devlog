Hiding Out in the Open: Marianne Jean-Baptiste in “Secrets & Lies”

MUBI Notebook · 2026-06-08 08:00 UTC

Secrets &amp; Lies (Mike Leigh, 1996). The woman in the black suit and gem earrings first appears as one among dozens of intoning faces captured from afar. Her calm eyes are fastened on a program, voice blended in hymn at the gravesite of the unidentified deceased. But as “How Great Thou Art” continues, she ceases to sing. Instead, she fixes on the middle distance, tears trickling down her cheeks. Hers is a private grief that cannot be readily and consistently integrated into the congregation’s lament. Surely she has her reasons. *** Black British life had seldom factored into the everyday dramas of Mike Leigh before 1996’s Secrets &amp; Lies . Leigh’s film, among the most commercially and critically successful of his career,is driven by the dogged efforts of Hortense Cumberbatch (Marianne Jean-Baptiste), a young, Black, introverted optometrist, to uncover and make contact with her birth mother, Cynthia (Brenda Blethyn), a blowzy, woebegone factory worker who is also, much to Hortense’s amazement, white. In the wake of her adoptive mother’s off-screen death, Hortense finds herself increasingly and willingly pulled into Cynthia’s lonely life and chaotic family, which includes another illegitimate adult daughter (Claire Rushbrook), whom Cynthia has raised and alienated, and an unfailingly altruistic brother, Maurice (Timothy Spall), whose wife (Phyllis Logan) is infertile and resentful of her sister-in-law. Other revelations are dragged into the light over the course of the film but it is the unknown existence and racial identity of Hortense that most pivotally determine the narrative of Secrets &amp; Lies , providing the film with its reason for being. In Leigh’s cinema, characters belong as much to the actors playing them as to the director himself. As the filmmaker himself has made clear , his rigorous, much-mythologized method famously enlists “the actor as a contributing artist, not merely an interpreter.” His films, like those of his closest forebear John Cassavetes, have frequently been misconstrued as taped improvisations, warped by the whims of its performers. It is true that, for most of Leigh’s projects, actors conceive their characters from scratch, sometimes drawing from a list of people that they have known or encountered in their own lives. But the actors then work separately and with Leigh for months to refine personalities, professions, habits, and pasts down to the most humdrum detail, until they can confidently claim to know this fictive person from the inside out. Once this incubation period is over, the actor begins collaborating with other actors enmeshed in the same process to create relationships, deepen dynamics, and braid together their histories. Through extended and unrecorded improvisations, on-site rehearsals, and Leigh’s own external plotting, a story gradually crystallizes, though a proper screenplay tends to be put on paper only after the film is completed. Leigh’s actors shape, influence, and—in the truest, most organic sense—co-author their characters but, once on set, the actions these characters take and the choices they make are negotiated between the performers and Leigh, whose “decision-making and interventions inform every film, right across the board.” 1 The result is a work molded by many minds, a film equally scavenged and structured, its freewheeling characters held within narrative and relational parameters imposed by Leigh. After studying acting at the Royal Academy of Dramatic Art, Leigh decisively turned to directing and derived his filmic practice from his experiences helming productions on the London stage, a foundation felt in the vivid, often volatile intensity of his actors’ efforts. As Leigh puts it, “To get everything right for that one moment on film, that’s what interests me. You want the spontaneity of the theater to happen at that white-hot moment when the camera is rolling.” 2 Although Leigh’s films have one foot planted in the quotidian realm of kitchen-sink drama, the majority of his actors have retained a relatively heightened manner of performance, an anti-naturalism, that tends to exceed our slippery frame of cinematic realism, awing some critics—Ian Buruma aptly described Leigh’s climaxes as “theater, but with the dangerous edge of reality”—and repelling others, who have accused Leigh of condescending to his lower-class characters, vis-à-vis his actors and their warts-and-all portrayals. Called on repeatedly to defend his approach, Leigh can occasionally undersell the tension he generates by unleashing his restive, larger-than-life players into existing places and letting their bursts of behavior transform the physical world into a proscenium. His characters are “real people, like you and me,” he has claimed, but this doesn’t remotely get at the ferment and volubility of his actors and all that is delightfully, maddeningly, and diversely too much about the people who tend to populate his ever-expanding Britannia. How, then, does the presence of a Black protagonist complicate the notion of a typical Mike Leigh character, as symbolized in the slovenly, spluttering form of David Thewlis’s Johnny, the irate white man stripped of charm and armed with a serrated, mile-a-minute wit in Naked , the 1993 film released directly before Secrets &amp; Lies ? And if a Mike Leigh performance is synonymous with vigorous, uninhibited expression, what might the reserve of a performer in a Mike Leigh film consequently signify about her character’s subjectivity? Given everything known about Leigh’s working methods, Hortense must be regarded as the creation of both the director and Marianne Jean-Baptiste, the RADA-trained actress who first imagined and invented her and whose muted yet emotionally variegated performance does not function as nor conform to our expectations of a typical Mike Leigh performance. Hortense’s blackness discernibly sets her apart from the film’s other main characters; as Jean-Baptiste notes , “She’s the subject [of the film] but is in the subtext” of all that is conveyed and concealed among its other denizens, her birth family. Yet Jean-Baptiste personifies and expands on the character’s difference in more artful ways, refusing to exceptionalize or oversimplify her. The performance instead hinges on modes of quiet and opacity that distinguish Jean-Baptiste from her fellow actors in the film and those of Leigh’s filmography. “The theory of difference is invaluable,” wrote Édouard Glissant in his appeal for the right to opacity, which doubles as an assertion of autonomy, on behalf of minorities. “But difference can still contrive to reduce things to the Transparent.” In Secrets &amp; Lies , Hortense fosters an unmistakable connection to the viewer yet eludes psychological transparency and refrains from complete knowability. It is Jean-Baptiste who, through subtle performance style and primary authorship of the character, carefully sets the terms by which Hortense will and will not be legible to those who surround her and those in the audience who are nonetheless magnetized to the actress’ every delicate move. *** Secrets &amp; Lies (Mike Leigh, 1996). The casting of the dark-skinned Marianne Jean-Baptiste as the mixed-race daughter of a white woman drew vocal dissension upon the release of Secrets &amp; Lies . “She didn’t look right,” wrote Karen Alexander in a decade-capping overview of Black British cinema in the 1990s, recognizing Jean-Baptiste’s significant talents but deeming her “phenotypically unconvincing” in the part. The performance anticipates this critique early on: when a too-kindly social worker (Lesley Manville) tells Hortense that it is perfectly feasible for her birth mother to be white, her reaction is halfway between a baffled scowl and a soundless scoff. Believing Jean-Baptiste as the daughter of Brenda Blethyn may require a suspension of disbelief for audiences seeking frictionless fidelity from Leigh’s film—not an altogether unreasonable expectation for a filmmaker who has resolutely stressed the verisimilitude of the social worlds he has chronicled. And yet this fidelity claim might be easier to overlook for those who are immediately in thrall to Jean-Baptiste’s mellow certitude in the role and her palpable, lived-in devotion to the character; the actress is not merely an inhabitant of Hortense’s reality but one of its chief architects. “People keep talking about the script, asking what attracted me to the role,” Jean-Baptiste said in 1996. “There was no role—what you’re attracted to is the process.” Jean-Baptiste’s preparation for Secrets &amp; Lies involved attending university several times a week for three months in order to fully understand Hortense’s optometry practice. The actress gravitated to the hidden minutiae of Leigh’s method, not just selecting the character’s professional, monochromatic wardrobe but also storing those clothes and intermittently residing in the apartment that would double as Hortense’s, finding herself able to account for all of the items in her trash. Through improvisations, she fleshed out conjoined backstories with fellow actors Michele Austin, who momentarily appears as Hortense’s friend, and Brian Bovell and Trevor Laird, whose roles as Hortense’s brothers were truncated after a subplot about the sale of their childhood home was excised from the final cut. Jean-Baptiste found possessing extensive knowledge about the character “liberating” in the sense that it “frees you up to just be” before the camera. This ease is discernible across Secrets &amp; Lies , where Jean-Baptiste is an unforced and self-effacing presence, her performance closer in spirit to the attentive if agonized reserve of Spall than the frenzied mannerisms of Blethyn, Rushbrook, and others. Then again, it is Spall’s Maurice whose torment is the focal point of the film’s purgative climax, in which he apoplectically brandishes the film’s title like a cudgel against his loved ones. Hortense is granted no such catharsis yet remains all the more fascinating for keeping her feelings and motivations so close to the vest. Jean-Baptiste reveals various shades of the character: her affability with her patients; her weary disinterest in her brothers’ briefly glimpsed squabbles; her rueful disappointment at mother’s sudden passing. But Hortense is mostly captured in contemplation, though the precise content of these musings is scarcely explicated. Quiet, something of a rare trait in Leigh’s films, is paramount to Jean-Baptiste’s power in the part. Hortense’s default manner evokes Kevin Quashie’s notion of quiet as an untapped form of expressivity within Black culture, one not synonymous with “silence or stillness,” but rather “a metaphor for the full range of one’s inner life—one’s desires, ambitions, hungers, vulnerabilities, fears.” Quashie continues: The idea of quiet, then, can shift attention to what is interior. This shift can feel like a kind of heresy if the interior is thought of as apolitical or inexpressive, which it is not: one’s inner life is raucous and full of expression, especially if we distinguish the term “expressive” from the notion of public. Indeed the interior could be understood as the source of human action—that anything we do is shaped by the range of desires and capacities of our inner life. Jean-Baptiste’s quiet facade embodies thought without clarifying its subject matter, suggesting the restless inquisitiveness that propels Hortense forward, in search of Cynthia. But what compels Hortense to continually entertain Cynthia’s company past the point of their excruciating introduction and to develop and nurture a bond with a woman who, despite their shared DNA, is Hortense’s polar opposite in innumerable ways? The loss of Hortense’s adoptive mother, with whom she attests to having a normal, loving relationship but whom she claims to never really have known, is too simplistic an explanation. Both Leigh and Jean-Baptiste avoid rationalizing Hortense along these lines; there are no expository monologues about the derangement of grief nor the loneliness of the adopted child. Ashley Clark argues that “the film doesn’t really explore how Hortense processes the monumental upending of her own identity, how she reacts to the newly complicated nature of her own blackness in relation to her existing family and community.” And yet this lack of exploration arguably befits a fundamentally ambivalent character. What informs Hortense’s desire to not just find but know her white mother is ungraspable and obscure, opaque in that it is a choice deprived of a single dramatically-expedient reason. An emblematic moment of the performance occurs in a car as Hortense drives Cynthia home following their first meeting. Hortense reveals to Cynthia that she has known about her adoption since childhood, when her late mother revealed it to her on a flight home from Barbados. “Were you upset?” Cynthia asks Hortense. The latter responds, in a tone at once evasive and matter-of-fact that she “just looked out at the clouds,” an elision that refuses to dictate the tenor of this pivotal moment in the character’s life and preserves the privacy of young Hortense’s response. Cynthia does not press the inquiry further and, even by the film’s end, we never totally grasp what being adopted might mean—or have once meant—to Hortense, how this knowledge (and the years spent holding this knowledge in the front or back of one’s mind) informs her search to locate Cynthia. This is neither the first or the last time she will elude apprehension. What matters to Hortense—and to Jean-Baptiste—is her quest to know, rather than be known. Per Glissant, “For the time being, perhaps, give up this old obsession with discovering what lies at the bottom of natures.” *** Secrets &amp; Lies (Mike Leigh, 1996). It was Jean-Baptiste’s decision to sit beside Blethyn during Hortense and Cynthia’s first meeting in an empty Central London tearoom, an awkward arrangement that gives Secrets &amp; Lies its centerpiece scene and one of its signature images, that of Black daughter and white mother elbow-to-elbow in a booth, faces forward, acutely alone in their shock and chagrin. Jean-Baptiste intuited that it would be easier for Hortense to show Cynthia the paperwork proving her parentage if the two were seated alongside each other. But the blocking would also spare Hortense the embarrassment of having to look at her disbelieving mother throughout the eight-minute conversation, shot in one unbroken take. A study in contrasts emerges during this extended two-shot, a divergence not solely of fictitious dispositions but of acting styles; it is the prototypical Mike Leigh performance sitting next to its inverse. Blethyn’s wide-eyed recollection of the repressed encounter that produced Hortense dominates the scene but the eye keeps shifting leftward to Jean-Baptiste and the nuanced portrait of bemused discomfort that she sketches here with her patient waiting, gentle prodding, and downward stares. The lachrymose fervor of Blethyn’s style is recognizably discordant with her costar’s equanimity and the stunning incompatibility of this pair does not augur a genuine connection between mother and daughter. Hortense’s solitude is keenest in the company of others. The clear exception is an outlying scene that is one of the film’s most beloved, a dialogue between Hortense and another Black Londoner, her longtime friend, Austin’s Dionne. Over dinner and wine, the duo discuss their sexual pursuits, their mothers, and the discontents of their upbringings, complete with Hortense’s comical impersonation of a Caribbean neighbor. Their banter is fast, free, and relaxed; at one point, the scrunching-up of lips is all they require to understand one another. This is a different Hortense—not necessarily a “realer” or more “authentic” one, but a looser and funnier, more unfiltered and meditative one. We will seldom see this side of Hortense again—except when she is with Cynthia. Once Hortense starts socializing with her mother, Jean-Baptiste complicates our assumptions about where—and with whom—the character can find comfort. Hortense’s characteristic reticence remains strikingly dissimilar from Cynthia’s too-muchness, but she is not only reticent. There is a girlish giddiness to their handful of outings, even a sense of delight that Jean-Baptiste conveys as she pulls faces, imitates Sylvester Stallone, and giggles at a hungry Cynthia’s malapropic declaration that “I’m ravishing!” What exactly is Hortense doing here? Feeding an outsider’s curiosity about this white woman and her working-class world? Enjoying the flattery that Cynthia doles out about Hortense’s beauty and professional success? Honoring a personal imperative to relate to the only one of her mothers still living? Leigh and Jean-Baptiste never resolve this query, which is why the growing geniality of the scenes between Hortense and Cynthia comes as such a surprise. And yet for all of their merriment, they are still women adjusting to implausible roles, and the self-conscious strain of their pauses say as much as their gasps of laughter. “Human behaviors are fractal in nature,” Glissant observed. Recall, too, Hortense’s complicated reaction when Cynthia finally identifies herself as her mother in that tearoom, the mixture of appreciation and unease that sifts through Jean-Baptiste’s face without settling into something straightforward. Her response is unfixed, as is her nature. *** The climax of Secrets &amp; Lies unfolds over the course of an explosive 21st birthday party for Cynthia’s daughter Roxanne, held at Maurice’s home. Hortense, pretending to be a workmate of Cynthia’s, attempts to use her politesse as a shield but only recedes further into herself, her discomfiture resembling a kind of nausea. (Notice that the gems hanging prominently from her ears are the same ones worn to her mother’s funeral.) When Cynthia, without warning, drops the bombshell about her newfound daughter, Hortense is forced to disclose her real identity. The character is not central to the recriminations and rapprochements that follow but becomes a silent, steely bystander to the threatened breakdown of her maternal family. Hortense noticeably chooses not to participate, an observer to the eruptions. At one point, she apathetically shrugs away Maurice’s concerned pats on the arm. It is only when Cynthia reveals to Roxane the long-withheld identity of her father, by her account “a nice man” studying abroad from America, that Hortense intervenes, coldly asking her mother, “Was my father a nice man?” The intentionally barbed question does its damage. But Hortense takes Cynthia in her arms, the embrace a mercy. She takes the path forward. Leigh’s film ends with Hortense welcomed into the family, testing the waters with Roxane in the backyard of the terraced house she shares with Cynthia. The pair admit to the weirdness of this arrangement but the mood between them remains light and airy as they opt to believe that something worthwhile and sustainable may be fostered between them. Cynthia’s reckless reveal and the pain subsequently restrained by Hortense are only minutes in the past. But Hortense, at least, is willing to see this experiment through. Hortense is not so much an enigma as an explorer, one whose motives are entirely her own, a seeker of connection who evades absolutes and declines to explain herself. Jean-Baptiste, never once raising her voice and hardly ever losing her composure, markedly expands our preconceptions about what a Mike Leigh performance might look and sound like. Other Leigh stalwarts (Ruth Sheen, Imelda Staunton) share Jean-Baptiste’s knack for unfurling a pause with currents of thought and feeling, but I can’t say that I wonder about their characters in the way I do Hortense. (I want to believe that she’d hit it off with Sally Hawkins’s Poppy, a zestier character but one who sends out similar rays of decency in 2008’s Happy-Go-Lucky .) Jean-Baptiste’s performance is a balancing act between actorly charisma and all too human ambiguity, between narratively-ordained blackness and a refusal to represent the Blackness of this particular British woman as innate, predetermined, monolithic. At all times, Jean-Baptiste protects and preserves the introversion, modesty, and quiet of the character she brought into being. In 1997, Jean-Baptiste became the first Black British actress to be nominated for an Academy Award for her performance in Secrets &amp; Lies . (Although Blethyn and Jean-Baptiste share virtually equal screen time, the latter was campaigned as a supporting actress. 3 ) That same year, the thirty-year-old actress was excluded from an all-white delegation of young actors invited by the organization British Screen to attend the Cannes Film Festival, where Leigh’s film had won the Palme d’Or the year prior. Jean-Baptiste did not let the slight go quietly, telling The Guardian , “It is a shame on Britain. I see myself as British and I want to be celebrated by Britain.” Despite the historic recognition, this slight and the subsequent paucity of substantial opportunities for Jean-Baptiste in the decades since her breakthrough are testaments to how habitually disregarded Black interiority remains onscreen. She has found heftier parts on the London stage, including a recent triumph in an Ivo Van Hove revival of Arthur Miller’s All My Sons . But the British film industry and Hollywood, which has kept the actress in workaday rotation since her move to the States in the 2000s, have failed to plumb her greater depths. The exception to this trajectory was facilitated through the director whom the actress has said she would pay to hire her. Almost 30 years after Secrets &amp; Lies , Jean-Baptiste reunited with Leigh for Hard Truths (2024), an unshakable showcase for the actress, whose middle-class housewife, the inaptly named Pansy, carries anger like a glove that has been superglued to the hand. Searching for a skirmish with strangers and loved ones alike, Pansy cannot help but pucker her lips in disgust at the faintest sign of contentment in a society that has, for reasons beyond her control, denied her all its comforts and joys. Jean-Baptiste badgers and bellows with a fury first hilarious then haunting, rattling the cage that a lifetime of dashed hopes has raised around Pansy. (She also rekindles and spikes the rapport first sparked with her Secrets &amp; Lies bestie Michele Austin, majestic as Pansy’s forbearing and magnanimous sister.) Operating light years away from the self-containment of her breakthrough role, Jean-Baptiste sets a new criterion in Leigh’s canon of modern misanthropes and delivers the sort of performance around which acting careers are immortalized. Still, it is Hortense that feels like the rarer feat, the figure easier to neglector underestimate. Jean-Baptiste looks largely unaffected by the templates of her costars and predecessors in Leigh’s ensembles. But she also seems impervious to the straitjacketing expectations of cinematic relatability that have narrowed our tolerance for those who are not instantly apprehensible, whose lives are not mirrors reflecting back our own existence with crystal clarity. The actress ensures that Hortense remains irreducible, a character who rivets less for what she reveals than for the promise that beneath her quiet reserve spins a world unto itself. 1. Times and Tides: Tsui Hark Discusses His Career

MUBI Notebook · 2026-06-04 08:00 UTC

Illustration by Alix Pentecost Farren. There are few blockbuster filmmakers who are as industrious as Tsui Hark. A leading figure of the Hong Kong New Wave who has scaled the highest peaks of his national cinema, this prodigious multi-hyphenate has amassed more than 200 credits as either director, producer, writer, occasional actor, or some combination of all four. Often described as China’s answer to Steven Spielberg, Tsui was responsible for bringing Hollywood-style special effects to Hong Kong—literally, in the case of Zu: Warriors from the Magic Mountain (1983), for which he flew in a number of VFX artists who had worked on Star Wars (1977) and Tron (1982). Tsui has always operated at the vanguard of cinematic technologies. To watch his post-millennial output is to be dazzled by the blockbuster possibilities of digital ones and zeroes. Like Spielberg, much of his work as director is confined to the past or the realms of fantasy, but unlike his American contemporary, whose work this century is suffused with political critique, Tsui’s most recent epics of historical ( The Battle at Lake Changjin , 2021) or literary ( Legends of the Condor Heroes: The Gallants , 2025) populism are pointedly careful not to upset the nationalist apple cart. (Ahead of our conversation, even my questions were rigorously vetted.) Back in the day, Tsui would throw such caution to the wind: picture the burning tank that features prominently in A Better Tomorrow III: Love and Death in Saigon (1989), released just four months after the Tiananmen Square Massacre; or the spiritual turmoil of China’s cultural icon Wong Fei-hung in Once Upon a Time in China (1991), the film that made Jet Li a superstar. Then there’s Dangerous Encounter – 1st Kind (1980), a startlingly nihilistic youth movie that was banned by the state Censorship Unit for its political violence. While Tsui has yet to make another film as confrontational as Dangerous Encounter , he has made up for it with the genre-hopping breadth of his output. As director, he leaps between comedy ( Aces Go Places 3 , 1984; The Chinese Feast , 1995) and time-bending romance ( Love in the Time of Twilight , 1995), between fantastical melodrama ( Green Snake , 1993), dizzying action ( The Blade , 1995; Time and Tide , 2000), and whatever Tri-Star (1996) is. As a producer, he quickly became notorious for his meddlesome oversight, while simultaneously scoring smash hit after smash hit. John Woo reaped the rewards of his stewardship with A Better Tomorrow (1986), its 1987 sequel, and The Killer (1989)—all produced by Tsui—before the pair fell out, while the legendary King Hu lasted barely a month on The Swordsman (1990) under Tsui’s watchful eye. Peking Opera Blues (Tsui Hark, 1986). Tsui is of an age at which his most notable films are receiving anniversary restorations, with longstanding gaps in his expansive filmography finally beginning to be filled on home video. His 1984 masterpiece Shanghai Blues screened in Cannes in 2024 for its 40th, while the uncut version—with missing elements sourced from Tsui’s personal VHS copy—of Dangerous Encounter – 1st Kind was recently released on Blu-ray in France. (A US disc that uses the film’s alternate title Don’t Play With Fire is imminent.) To great astonishment, last year also saw the news that the home video label Shout! Studios had somehow gained the keys to one of cinema’s holy grails: the Golden Princess library, whose knotty business arrangements had kept many Hong Kong classics out of distribution for decades. With the floodgates now open, a number of Tsui’s films as producer and director are finally beginning to see the light of day, including the gender-bending masterwork often cited as his greatest, Peking Opera Blues (1986). Add to that Criterion’s recent 4K release of The Blade , hot on the heels of their 2021 boxset of the Once Upon a Time in China films, and it’s steadily becoming a great time to be a fan of Tsui Hark’s endlessly creative cinematic visions. With my questions approved, I jumped on a video call with Tsui, whose energy belied his 75 years. Even with my allotted 60 minutes stretched to 75, an attempt at a career interview with so much ground to cover was probably a fool’s errand, and I ran out of time before getting to some personal favorites like his Jean-Claude Van Damme-starring, stylistic blitzkrieg Knock Off (1998), or his incandescent ghost story Missing (2008). Great company, Tsui spoke in English, and this interview has been edited for clarity. NOTEBOOK: Let’s start at the very beginning of your filmmaking career. I’m curious about your experience studying film in Texas. You were in the US for a number of years. How did that come about? TSUI HARK: Going to America was unusual. I didn’t have any money, so I was always in crisis. Every semester while I was in school there was a financial alarm. I would work for a summer and by the time I returned to school the money was already spent. I would always be looking for a part-time job, for somewhere to stay. It was a very unstable situation, and I was skeptical about whether I would be able to finish my studies. I was also concerned about how much I was learning. So in my senior year at university, one semester before graduation, I started working in New York City, at a place called New York Films. They were a documentary rental and production company that always seemed to be in financial difficulty. But I was happy to be earning a living working in film. One day I found out that I didn’t actually graduate from school; I didn’t get my degree. So I had to go back and finish my work. I thought a degree was very important, until later I found out that it didn’t matter at all. People look at your abilities rather than the record of your studies. A career in filmmaking means that you don’t have to worry about making a living, that you can spend your time dreaming about your own worlds. I went to the States because I wanted to get into the film industry. The best result would be that I could become a director. When I left school, I didn’t know whether I should return to New York, because I had a job there, or go back to Hong Kong. NOTEBOOK: Was the plan always to return to Hong Kong eventually to make films? TSUI: I went to America because it seemed like that’s where all the decisions were made, but I had to return to Hong Kong because that’s where all my ideas came from. Hong Kong was the source of my inspiration, it was in my blood. NOTEBOOK: You must have been watching a lot of American and world cinema as part of your studies. What were you especially fond of, and what effect do you think this exposure and training had on your sensibilities as a filmmaker? TSUI: You can’t exclude things and expect to become a unique and original entity. We are mutually influenced. Cinema began by someone experimenting with a series of projected images to create the illusion of reality. There’s so much history there that it’s impossible to feel that you are original, and the path you are on as a filmmaker is the sum total of all the emotions and experiences you’ve had as a viewer. Being yourself doesn’t mean that you have to exclude everybody else. You have all these wonderful directors that made movies that you really love. Are you influenced by them? Of course. But is your filmmaking really affected by them? I’m not sure. In my opinion, you can never do what they did. They did wonderful things that, even across your entire career, you can never hope to attain. You can spend your life loving their work, but sorry, it doesn’t mean that you can do the same things yourself. The films I loved at school weren’t the commercial, popular movies. I was much more influenced by underground, experimental cinema. This was the kind of work that had courage, and the boldness of spirit to fight against tradition. Then later you find out that this is just a process that you have to go through, and that you’ve learned from the process. But I do still love a lot of the films that I first encountered back then. I was very affected by the work of Maya Deren. Meshes of the Afternoon [1943] is very original, and very passionate. Even today, I don’t understand what it means, but this is a film that has its own meaning, that gets its message across not through dialogue but through images and emotional involvement. When you see it, you become very affected by it. Of course, there are other wonderful filmmakers like Kurosawa, Fellini, Bertolucci, Hitchcock, but someone I really want to mention is Leni Riefenstahl. NOTEBOOK: Wow. Okay. TSUI: She affected me a lot because I never realized that documentary could be made that way. For a while, I thought I could be a news reporter instead of a dramatic filmmaker. I thought I could be a very good documentary filmmaker. Even today, I think the same. It’s something I really think about. Zu: Warriors from the Magic Mountain (Tsui Hark, 1983). NOTEBOOK: That fusion of Eastern and Western influences was made explicit with Zu: Warriors from the Magic Mountain , which saw you return to the US to hire various special effects teams, including those who worked on Star Wars . The film is often cited as introducing American-style FX to Hong Kong cinema. Tell me about the genesis of this international collaboration. Was it born out of a need to solve a specific set of problems? TSUI: It was really ambitious thinking. The Hong Kong film industry at that time was really restricted by budgetary limitations. You could never think about doing something like Star Wars because you wouldn’t have the money. So, if someone offers the opportunity to try something even close to that, it’s a miracle. And that miracle happened to me. I was given the chance by Mr. Raymond Chow of Golden Harvest. He gave me the opportunity to do anything I wanted to do, probably because he was thinking of different ways in which he could grow his company. But the biggest thanks have to go to someone called Liu Liang-hua. She was behind the whole thing. I don’t know if you’ve heard of her? She was the one who found Bruce Lee in America, and she lent me credibility with Raymond Chow by standing behind me. I took advantage of that opportunity and it opened a door to a different stage in my career. In Hong Kong, in our industry, there was no tradition of post-production work when it came to things like special effects and optical work. It had never been done before in our industry. So I thought that I needed to knock on the doors of people who had done things like that in Hollywood. Hollywood felt like something that you would never be able to get access to. It was somewhere far from my world. But they were extremely friendly. They came over to Hong Kong to help me out with Zu for a period of time. Not for the whole film, but during that time I learned so much. I realized that some things can be done in a certain way, and that other things can be done that have never been done before. That was the inspiration that I got from these people. Zu had a lot of shots to deal with. They came over to help me with some of them, but it wasn’t enough. So I had to discover other ways to solve some problems. I made a lot of mistakes, and those mistakes became jokes in the industry that were passed around. But I thought it was worth it, because you have to go through that process in order to learn from your mistakes. It opened my eyes to how motion pictures could be made in a very silly way—silly, but wonderful. Once Upon a Time in China (Tsui Hark, 1991). NOTEBOOK: You returned to this world in 2001 with The Legend of Zu , this time embracing digital effects technology, which has defined the aesthetic of much of your work since the millennium. Do you relish the freedoms afforded by CGI, or are you nostalgic for the textures and challenges of practical effects? TSUI: I’m frequently asked about artificial intelligence. Do I think of A.I. as an enemy or a friend? I think we’re simply facing a different kind of invention. I remember when I first got a computer, people used to comment that this was the beginning of me cutting myself off from my friends. Then, not long after, everyone started carrying cell phones, and they started looking at those instead of seeing the world. I think whenever we’re faced with something like that, you can’t let it affect your life, or let it affect your whole career. They are not gods. They are not holy spirits that you worship in the temple. You can make use of the technology at that specific time, but it can also backfire because these things are inconsistent and quickly become obsolete. Once everyone has access to the software or the apps, then suddenly everyone can do it. It’s a double-edged sword. I think the most important thing is that you don’t make technology the most important element in making a movie. People talk about cinema being diminished day by day because of the competition from other media. The theatrical business is going down, and maybe cinema will soon be history. But I think the creative side of movies depends too much on a personal vision, not the technical side of things. CGI has nothing to do with someone’s capability to make a good film or not. When you can’t find the money to use these technologies, you find an alternative path to walk. Sometimes, that ends up being a better way. A lot of great films were born that way: the filmmakers couldn’t do something because of a lack of money and conditions, but then they found an alternative solution, leading to a classic that we keep looking at and talking about. NOTEBOOK: Speaking of A.I. and classic films, what did you think of the news last year that the China Film Foundation intends to update Once Upon a Time in China and all the Bruce Lee films using artificial intelligence? TSUI: A.I. can help to make a film look better and get rid of artefacts due to poor preservation. But the most important thing is that you have to keep Bruce Lee clean, and exactly the same as he was before. You cannot change Bruce Lee. One of the problems with A.I. is that it creates something different from what it was in the beginning. You don’t see the reality of the situation any more. That’s one of the big worries with A.I.: its ability to separate you from reality. It can stop you from distinguishing what is real or not. Some people may not care, but it’s scary when you’re not able to tell whether that’s actually Bruce Lee or a computer-generated simulation. But it’s become a very common tool. As soon as A.I. starts replacing creativity, that will be the death of the industry and all manner of professions. Dangerous Encounters: 1st Kind (Tsui Hark, 1980). NOTEBOOK: Zu: Warriors from the Magic Mountain was a critical hit, but it was a real departure from your first three films. Dangerous Encounters: 1st Kind , especially, is a ferocious movie. Where did the rage in that film come from? TSUI: Those feelings were not unique to me. There was a lot of turmoil and instability in the world back then; a lot of questioning of the need to adhere to regulations and traditions. The kind of rage in that film exists in everyone, to different degrees. If you wanted to say something about the world at that time, that was a natural expression. Back then, I was looking for something that could fulfill my urge to make movies. I felt like I was becoming an unexceptional person by making unexceptional movies. So I had to do something about it. I wanted to express my innate personality, making audiences share my compassion and my feelings through provocation. I still feel that way now, it’s just taken on a different means of expression. The films may not be saying “kill everything,” but it’s still in my nature to want to destroy things. If something bothers you a lot, you want to get rid of it. When I look back at Dangerous Encounters now, I think it wants for depth. The anger that I wanted to share with the audience would have lasted longer in their memories if the film had a little more depth. Every time you go to see a movie, you should be getting a new perspective on who you are. That has to be the most important thing about the work you create. You want to reach an audience’s humanity, to trigger an angry or romantic response that helps them remember who they are. We're Going to Eat You (Tsui Hark, 1980). NOTEBOOK: Like your previous film We’re Going to Eat You [1980], it’s a very political picture. How do you think the politics in your filmmaking have evolved over the years? TSUI: We’re Going to Eat You is not a serious film at all. I was just making fun of the horror genre. It’s a comedy! It’s not philosophical and doesn’t have any deep meaning, it was just a parody. When I made that movie, I didn’t really pay much attention to its commercial potential. I was just challenging everyone’s values when it comes to looking at a movie like this. But then I thought, Is this what people are really looking for in their entertainment, or can we give them something else? It was adapted from the work of a famous Chinese writer, and was about people eating people unconsciously. They’re killing each other without knowing why they’re doing it. It was about villagers who cannot accept outsiders, and so they make them disappear. And that’s related to what is happening now. Some people can’t accept that things are changing, they want to keep the old ways. I’m not implying that the new ways are good or bad, but we need to evolve with the times. We need to live with the processes of civilization and humanity, otherwise rigidity and conservatism will destroy our nature. I’m talking about all these things in the movie, but it was totally unserious. NOTEBOOK: Shortly after Zu , you made two of your greatest films in Shanghai Blues and Peking Opera Blues , the first of which recently celebrated its fortieth birthday with a new restoration. Did you watch a lot of screwball comedies while you were studying in the US, and were you thinking of these American films from the 1930s and 1940s while you were developing Shanghai Blues ? TSUI: I’ve always been fond of comedy as a genre. I’m a comedy person. I watched a lot of screwball comedies, Charlie Chaplin, Buster Keaton, Woody Allen, and then a lot of classic Japanese, Chinese, and Indian comedies too. They’re really entertaining. But Shanghai Blues originated from an experience I had with a friend, who told me about a singer who lost her diamond rings on stage. She was meant to start singing, but then looked at her fingers and saw that her rings were missing, so she started screaming instead. I thought that was very funny. I kept thinking about this character, wondering why she was on stage and how she lost her rings. Soon enough, the story came to me when I decided to set it during the Second World War. That period gives us an opportunity to look at human nature in a global sense. Once I had that, the story started to take care of itself: what was this woman’s reality? How did she make her livelihood in this world? That’s the great thing about creativity, it all comes from you. Once you’ve got a trigger that makes everything click into place, it all pours out. And that’s how Shanghai Blues came about. It doesn’t relate to the diamond ring at all, or the scream on the stage, because it turned out I didn’t have room to put that in the film once the story had taken off by itself. There were a lot of problems with that film though, because it was supposed to be a low-budget movie. Which means there are lots of scenes, locations, and special effects that you have to give up. I didn’t have the money to do any special effects properly, so I had to do them very cheaply, using things like papier-mâché and cardboard miniatures. But that was really interesting; that’s real movie-making. You make every effort you can to share an emotion with the audience. The world needs this film’s humour and optimism to make it more lovable and livable. The world is so chaotic, it’s hard to tolerate sometimes. Shanghai Blues (Tsui Hark, 1984). NOTEBOOK: Sylvia Chang and Sally Yeh are wonderful in Shanghai Blues . Do you prefer writing for women? TSUI: I don’t like to think of it that way, because it will limit my ideas. But it’s a bit like baking a cake. You don’t know how it’s going to taste in the end. Sometimes the women’s parts turn out more tasty than the men’s parts. When people tell me I write better parts for women than men, it makes me feel like I need to work harder on my writing for men! NOTEBOOK: Peking Opera Blues is fascinating for its exploration of gender identity, something that recurs throughout your filmography. Tell me a little about your interest in subverting gender roles. TSUI: I know people in real life who are not defined by social criteria. Some people say that a woman should be like this and a man should be like that, but in real life they’re not! And these unique people I know who refuse to be defined that way are usually so interesting. People who don’t follow the rules or go by social standards have a unique attitude towards life, and I respect that. So I don’t think of myself as subverting anything. NOTEBOOK: You had some huge early successes as a producer at Film Workshop, including A Better Tomorrow and A Chinese Ghost Story [1987]. And you’ve earned a reputation over the years for being very hands-on when you’re producing. Tell me a little about your relationships with directors when you have your producing hat on. TSUI: I’ve always been warned about my hands-on habit. I used to think it was a necessity of my role as a producer, but I’ve tried to get out of the … what should I call it? … the curse of my habit. I never know if I’m a good producer or not, because sometimes the projects turn out wonderful, and other times I worry that I’ve ruined them. But I do know what I’m talking about. If there’s something I shouldn’t have put my hands on, I try to get out of it. And then if I think I need to do something about something, then I’ll just do it. It’s often hard to distinguish which films I should get fully involved with and which ones I shouldn’t. A Better Tomorrow III: Love and Death in Saigon (Tsui Hark, 1989). NOTEBOOK: You produced some incredible films for John Woo, before parting company on A Better Tomorrow III: Love and Death in Saigon , which you directed yourself. Woo later directed his own variation with Bullet in the Head [1990]. It seems there are a lot of conflicting stories out there about the reasons for your split, so I was wondering if you could talk a little about what happened there? TSUI: That incident involved a lot of outside factors. John Woo had gained a great reputation by that point, and he was able to start looking at going into international productions. Meanwhile, I was hugely entangled in an industry production crisis that was going on at the time. He didn’t want to do episode three. He thought we’d already reached the end of it. And I agreed! But I was tempted to say more about this character by looking into his past. Then I realized I was wrong. It didn’t turn out how I expected. John Woo had his own way of developing films with his company, and that’s why we separated, in a way. Of course, there’s no separation without negative feelings. That came about from him thinking I was interfering with his creativity, that sort of thing. And I agree that I did have that problem. It happens to everybody in the creative industries, though. You never know whose idea is right or wrong until you see the results. A few years later we got together and talked about it, and we felt that this was now in the past and there were more important things coming up. Later, I tried to do as much as possible in terms of helping him out. We managed to maintain our friendship, and we both feel, just like the characters in A Better Tomorrow , that we’re brothers. NOTEBOOK: From the climaxes of Peking Opera Blues and Once Upon a Time in China , to The Blade and Time and Tide —you’ve directed some breathtaking action set pieces. Tell me a little about how you put these sequences together? Do you storyboard? TSUI: I only use storyboards when I have to use special effects, because people need to know the budget and what the technical requirements will be. Otherwise the producer, or the person in charge of the money, gets very insecure. You need storyboards when you want to create a spectacular shot. I always tell the storyboard artists that spectacular doesn’t mean there needs to be thousands of people, spectacular can just mean a close-up of an eye. A pupil alone can do a lot of things to an audience’s response. There have been different trends in action throughout the history of the genre. Every new trend creates a new sensation, and this has to be designed by necessity. Each one creates a new reality. Look at Jurassic Park [1993]. Suddenly dinosaurs are in front of you, walking around. You want to create difficult action that has never been seen before in the movies. Like when Jackie Chan slides down thirty floors from the roof of a building. You’d never seen that before. The romantic element of the action itself is also hugely important. People want to be a part of the action, and there’s often a romantic element that causes people to love the scene. But everything always changes, according to the times, because so many people try to imitate the new trend. And then it dies off because people have seen too much of it. You have to try to start a new trend with every movie, because you’re always going to face the same problem. The Blade (Tsui Hark, 1995). NOTEBOOK: The Blade is an astonishing action picture, and a film that seems to resurrect the nihilistic energy of Dangerous Encounter . It’s your spin on Chang Cheh’s The One-Armed Swordsman [1967], but it’s a film that seems set on tearing down and brutalizing what came before. How did the project evolve into something so dark and subversive? TSUI: It’s interesting because I wanted to go back to a documentary style. It wasn’t easy, because so much relies on your cameraman being able to catch a sudden moment. It’s like being in the middle of a riot, where everything relies on capturing the moment a policeman strikes a demonstrator amid the chaos. It’s not something you can plan. But with a movie, you have to plan, because otherwise the performers won’t know what’s going on, and that’s dangerous. So you have to imitate unplannedness while actually planning very carefully. We ended up using an intercom system where I was talking to the cameraman, telling him to catch a glimpse of the guy emerging from the alley swinging his sword, before quickly panning away to the girl running away. It was chaotic, but fun. I didn’t have any dialogue in the script. The dialogue was invented by the performers, and that was very challenging for them. They had to figure out what to say, which was particularly hard in the emotional scenes. It was an experiment, and other people have tried it since, with very good results. NOTEBOOK: Your films have been getting bigger and bigger in recent years. Do you have any desire to make smaller-scale pictures like those from earlier in your career? TSUI: I always want to go back to low-budget filmmaking. The industry is shrinking and it’s a dire situation. People are suffering. The theatrical business is sinking, with competition coming from other media. The cinema circuit is shrinking. It’s a very bad time for movies. But nothing is worse than war. War is a terrible thing to happen to human beings. Although I do think that creativity always emerges after suffering.
